# 01 · Before You Arrive

> **Goal of this stage:** Get your paperwork bulletproof, pre-check your degree, and start German — *before* you leave your home country. What you do here saves months later.

Most of the pain immigrants report in Germany traces back to missing documents and unrecognised qualifications. The single highest-leverage thing you can do is **prepare the paper trail early**, because German offices are strict, German-only, and slow to correct mistakes.

---

## 1. Gather and secure your core documents

Collect originals **and** multiple certified copies of:

- Passport (valid well beyond your intended stay)
- Birth certificate
- Marriage certificate (if applying with a spouse)
- University degree(s) and full transcripts
- Professional/vocational certificates
- Employment reference letters
- Proof of health insurance for the entry period
- Passport photos (biometric, German spec: 35×45 mm)

**Do this before you fly.** Re-issuing a birth certificate from abroad once you're in Germany is painful.

---

## 2. Certified translations & apostilles

German authorities generally require documents in **German**, and foreign public documents often need an **apostille** (or full legalisation for non–Hague Convention countries).

- **Certified translation (*beglaubigte Übersetzung*)** — must be done by a translator sworn/authorised by a German court. Translations done in your home country are sometimes rejected; check with the German embassy first.
- **Apostille** — an international certification (under the Hague Convention) proving your document is genuine. Obtain it from the competent authority in the *issuing* country. Countries outside the Convention need embassy legalisation instead.

> 💡 Budget several weeks and real money for this. It is one of the most common sources of visa delays.

---

## 3. Pre-check your degree recognition (this is critical)

Before you rely on your qualification for a visa or job, verify how Germany views it.

- **anabin database** — Germany's official database (run by the ZAB) rating foreign institutions and degrees as **H+ (recognised), H+/- , or H-**. If your university and degree are listed as H+ with the right equivalence, your visa and Blue Card path is dramatically smoother.
- **Statement of Comparability (*Zeugnisbewertung*)** from the **ZAB (Zentralstelle für ausländisches Bildungswesen)** — a formal document evaluating your foreign higher-education degree. Often required for visas and job applications.

For **regulated professions** (doctor, nurse, teacher, lawyer, engineer, many crafts) recognition is **mandatory** before you can work — see [Step 10](10-recognition-of-qualifications.md). Start this months ahead: medical *Approbation* alone can take 4–12 months per state.

> ⚠️ **Deskilling is a real risk.** Germany's over-qualification rate for non-EU workers is just under **33%**, and among academics who trained outside the EU, **fewer than four in ten** end up in a job matching their qualifications. Getting recognition right is the main defence against this.

---

## 4. Start learning German now

You will hear "everyone speaks English" — in daily bureaucracy, healthcare, and most jobs, they do not.

- **67% of expats** find German difficult, but even A1–A2 before arrival transforms your first months.
- Target milestones that unlock later benefits: **B1** speeds up permanent residence and is the standard citizenship threshold; **A1** is often required for a spouse's family-reunification visa.
- Use Goethe-Institut, VHS online courses, or apps to reach at least A1 before you land.

See [Step 09](09-language-and-integration.md) for the full integration-course system.

---

## 5. Money & logistics checklist

- [ ] Proof of funds / **blocked account (*Sperrkonto*)** if required (mandatory for most student visas — ~€11,904/year as of recent rules; verify current figure)
- [ ] Book a **temporary place to live** for your first 2–4 weeks (Airbnb, serviced apartment, or a landlord who will register you) — you'll need an address for [Anmeldung](03-anmeldung.md)
- [ ] Open an international-friendly account (e.g. a neobank) you can use before you have a German address — see [Step 05](05-bank-account.md)
- [ ] Bring **cash** — Germany is still surprisingly cash-based
- [ ] Digital + physical copies of every document, stored redundantly

---

## ✅ Before-you-fly checklist

- [ ] Passport + certified copies of all civil/education documents
- [ ] Certified German translations arranged
- [ ] Apostilles / legalisation obtained
- [ ] Degree checked on **anabin**; ZAB *Zeugnisbewertung* ordered if needed
- [ ] Regulated-profession recognition **started** (if applicable)
- [ ] Visa appointment booked at the German embassy/consulate ([Step 02](02-visa-and-entry.md))
- [ ] German lessons underway (aim A1+)
- [ ] Temporary accommodation booked
- [ ] Proof of funds / blocked account ready
