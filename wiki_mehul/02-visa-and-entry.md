# 02 · Visa & Entry

> **Goal of this stage:** Pick the right entry route and get the visa that matches your situation. This is the longest-lead-time step — start it while doing [Step 01](01-before-you-arrive.md).

**EU/EEA/Swiss citizens:** you do **not** need a visa or residence permit — freedom of movement applies. You can skip straight to [Anmeldung](03-anmeldung.md). Everything below is for **non-EU (third-country) nationals**.

A few nationalities (USA, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, Israel, UK) may enter Germany visa-free and apply for their residence permit **from inside** the country. Everyone else applies for a **national visa (D-visa)** at a German embassy/consulate *before* travelling.

---

## The main routes compared

| Route | Best for | Key requirement | Notes |
|-------|----------|-----------------|-------|
| **EU Blue Card** | Graduates with a job offer | University degree + salary threshold + qualifying job | Fastest track to permanent residence |
| **Skilled Worker visa** (§18a/b) | Vocational or academic professionals with a job | Recognised qualification + job offer | Broader than Blue Card |
| **Chancenkarte (Opportunity Card)** | Job-seekers without an offer yet | Points-based system | Come and look for work legally |
| **Job Seeker visa** | Qualified professionals | Recognised degree + funds | 6-month search window |
| **Student visa** | University/prep-course students | Admission + blocked account | Can work limited hours |
| **Family Reunification** | Spouses/children of residents | Sponsor's status + (often) A1 German | See special rules below |

---

## 🔵 EU Blue Card (the flagship skilled route)

The Blue Card is the most advantageous work permit for degree-holders.

**Salary thresholds (from 1 January 2026):**
- **Standard occupations:** €50,700 gross/year
- **Shortage occupations, recent graduates, and qualifying IT specialists:** €45,934.20 gross/year

**Key points:**
- IT specialists can qualify **without a formal degree** if they have 3+ years of relevant experience.
- The office applies the threshold **in force on your employment start date**.
- **Bonuses do not count** toward the minimum — base salary must clear the bar.
- **Permanent residence** comes fast: after **27 months** of qualifying work with A1 German, or **21 months** with B1 German (pension contributions must be paid throughout).

---

## 🟡 Chancenkarte / Opportunity Card

Introduced under the 2023–2024 Skilled Immigration Act reforms, this **points-based** card lets qualified people come to Germany to **look for work** without a prior offer.

- Points awarded for qualifications, German/English language skills, age, and experience.
- Lets you take trial jobs and part-time work while searching.
- A modern alternative to the older **Job Seeker visa** (which gives ~6 months to find a job but no work rights during the search).

---

## 🎓 Student visa

- Requires an **admission letter** from a German university (or a *Studienkolleg* prep course).
- **Blocked account (*Sperrkonto*)** proving you can support yourself (~€11,904/year — verify the current figure).
- Students may work a limited number of days/hours per year alongside studies.
- After graduation you can switch to a **residence permit to seek work** (up to 18 months), then to a Blue Card / Skilled Worker permit.

---

## 👪 Family reunification

To bring a spouse/partner:

- The **sponsor** in Germany must have adequate income, housing, and the right residence status.
- The joining spouse usually must prove **basic German at A1 level** *before* the visa is issued (§30 AufenthG).
- **Important exemptions:** the A1 requirement is waived for spouses joining **skilled workers, EU Blue Card holders, and researchers**, and for nationals of visa-exempt countries (US, Canada, Australia, Japan, South Korea, etc.).
- Processing typically takes **3–6 months**, but in high-demand embassies (South Asia, parts of Africa) appointment waits alone can push it past a year.

---

## How the application works

1. **Book an embassy/consulate appointment early** — slots in high-demand countries fill months ahead.
2. Submit the **national visa (D-visa)** application with your document pack from [Step 01](01-before-you-arrive.md).
3. For skilled routes, your employer can request a **pre-approval (*Vorabzustimmung*)** from the Federal Employment Agency to shorten processing.
4. Receive the **D-visa** (usually valid 3–6 months) → travel to Germany.
5. Once in Germany, do your [Anmeldung](03-anmeldung.md), then convert the visa into a **residence permit** at the [Ausländerbehörde](08-residence-permit.md).

> 💡 The visa in your passport is only the *entry* document. The real, longer-term permit is issued **inside Germany** after you register.
