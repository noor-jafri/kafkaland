# 10 · Recognising Your Qualifications (Anerkennung)

> **Goal of this stage:** Get your foreign degree or professional qualification officially recognised so you can work at your real level — and avoid deskilling.

**Anerkennung** (recognition) is the process of having Germany formally evaluate your foreign qualification. For some jobs it's **mandatory**; for others it's optional but powerful. Getting this right is the single biggest defence against underemployment.

---

## Regulated vs. non-regulated professions

| | **Regulated professions** | **Non-regulated professions** |
|---|---|---|
| **Examples** | Doctor, nurse, teacher, lawyer, engineer (in some cases), many crafts | Most IT, business, science, and many academic roles |
| **Recognition** | **Mandatory** — you legally can't practise without it | **Optional** — but helps employers trust your qualification |
| **Process** | Formal equivalence assessment + sometimes exams | anabin check / ZAB Statement of Comparability often enough |

---

## The tools

- **anabin** — Germany's official database (run by the ZAB) rating foreign institutions/degrees (**H+, H+/-, H-**). Check yours **before** you rely on it.
- **ZAB Statement of Comparability (*Zeugnisbewertung*)** — a formal evaluation of your foreign higher-education degree; widely accepted by employers and offices.
- **"Recognition in Germany" portal (anerkennung-in-deutschland.de)** — the official one-stop guide and finder for the right recognition authority.
- **Recognition Partnership (*Anerkennungspartnerschaft*)** — since March 2024, lets skilled workers **start working while recognition is still pending**, under an agreement with the employer.

---

## Example: doctors (Approbation)

Recognition for regulated professions is heavy. For doctors:

- You need the **Approbation** (licence to practise).
- Requires proof of equivalence of your medical degree, plus **C1-level general German** and a **medical-language exam (*Fachsprachprüfung*)**.
- Processing typically runs **4–12 months per state**.
- If your training is judged only partially equivalent, you may face a **knowledge exam (*Kenntnisprüfung*)**.

Nurses, teachers, and lawyers face similarly demanding, state-specific paths.

---

## The deskilling risk (why this matters so much)

- Germany's **over-qualification rate for non-EU workers** is just under **33%** (below the EU average of 40%, but high).
- The **employment gap** between immigrants with German vs. foreign degrees is more pronounced in Germany than in most OECD countries.
- Among academics who graduated abroad (non-EU), **fewer than four in ten** hold a job matching their formal qualifications.
- A 2024 KOFA study found around **520,000 migrant women** unemployed in Germany, many overqualified.

**Takeaway:** the emphasis on **formal recognition** means your real skills can be discounted unless you document them the German way. Invest in recognition early.

---

## Practical steps

1. **Check anabin** for your institution and degree.
2. Use **anerkennung-in-deutschland.de** to find the **competent authority** for your profession and state.
3. For academic (non-regulated) roles, order a **ZAB Zeugnisbewertung**.
4. For **regulated** roles, start the formal equivalence process **months ahead** — and plan for language exams.
5. If you have a job offer, ask your employer about a **Recognition Partnership** so you can start working while it's pending.

- [ ] Determined whether your profession is regulated

- [ ] Ordered a **ZAB Statement of Comparability** (if useful)

- [ ] Started formal recognition early (regulated professions)
- [ ] Explored a **Recognition Partnership** with an employer
