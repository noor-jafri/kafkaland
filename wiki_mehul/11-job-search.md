# 11 · Finding a Job

> **Goal of this stage:** Land the employment contract — the destination of this whole journey. This means mastering German application norms, tapping the hidden job market, and navigating a market where bias exists.

By now you have (or are close to) a permit, a bank account, insurance, and recognised qualifications. This page is about converting all that into a signed **Arbeitsvertrag**.

---

## The German application (Bewerbung) — it has strict norms

German applications are more formal and structured than in many countries. A complete **Bewerbung** usually includes:

1. **Anschreiben (cover letter)** — one page, formal, tailored to the specific role; explains motivation and fit.
2. **Lebenslauf (CV)** — typically **tabular**, reverse-chronological, concise (1–2 pages), factual. Often includes date of birth and sometimes a **professional photo** (declining, but still common).
3. **Zeugnisse (certificates & references):**
   - **Arbeitszeugnis** — employer references written in a coded, formulaic German style (learn to read the "hidden grades").
   - Degree certificates + **recognition documents** ([Step 10](10-recognition-of-qualifications.md)).
   - Language certificates.

> 💡 Keep it **factual and complete**. German recruiters expect gaps to be explained and documents to be attached — not just a CV and a promise to "provide references on request."

## Where jobs are

- **Portals:** StepStone, Indeed, LinkedIn, XING (German-focused), and the Federal Employment Agency's **Arbeitsagentur** job board.
- **Make it in Germany** job board — aimed at international skilled workers.
- **Company career pages** — Germans often apply directly.
- **The hidden job market:** many roles are filled through **networks and referrals** before they're ever posted.

---

## Networking (more decisive than it looks)

German professional culture can feel reserved, but networks matter enormously:

- Attend **industry meetups, Meetup.com groups, and conferences**.
- Use **XING** and **LinkedIn** actively; German recruiters use both.
- **Informational interviews** and alumni networks open doors.
- Language *Stammtische* and expat groups double as professional networks.

---

## ⚠️ Hiring discrimination is documented — plan around it

Bias against foreign-sounding names is measurable:

- The classic **IZA field experiment** (Kaas & Manger) found a **German name raised callback probability by ~14%**, and **~24% at smaller firms**.
- A **2025 University of Siegen** study of 50,000+ applications found the German-named "Lukas Becker" got **67 replies per 100 applications**, vs 56 (Russian), 54 (Hebrew), 52 (Turkish), and just **36 (Arabic-sounding "Habiba Mahmoud")**.

**How to respond:**
- Apply **widely** and don't take early rejections personally.
- Lead with **recognised qualifications** and **German language certificates** to reduce uncertainty.
- Use **referrals** — a warm introduction bypasses CV screening bias.
- Larger firms and multinationals tend to discriminate less than small firms.
- Anti-discrimination advice centres (*Antidiskriminierungsstelle des Bundes*) can advise if you face clear discrimination.

## Interviews & offers

- Interviews are typically **structured and punctual** — arrive early, be direct, know the company.
- Expect questions on **notice periods, availability, and salary expectations** (Germans discuss salary ranges openly in interviews).
- Once you get an offer, check that the **salary clears your Blue Card threshold** if your permit depends on it ([Step 02](02-visa-and-entry.md)).
- Your permit may be **tied to skilled employment** — check conditions before changing jobs early on.

---

## Support services

- **Federal Employment Agency (Bundesagentur für Arbeit)** — free job placement and advice; the **Welcome Centers** and **IQ Network** support internationals.
- **Job centres** help with recognition, retraining, and language-for-work.
- **Chambers (IHK/HWK)** support recognition and vocational routes.
