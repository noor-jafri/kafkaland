# Having a Child in Germany (Nuremberg)

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Side quest — relevant whenever a child is born or expected, at any point after you're settled.
**Prerequisites:** [04-city-registration-anmeldung.md](04-city-registration-anmeldung.md), [06-health-insurance.md](06-health-insurance.md), and employment/self-employment status.
**Unlocks:** Nothing further — this is a standalone terminal side quest.

## Overview

Germany has a comparatively generous (if bureaucratically dense) system of protections and cash benefits around childbirth, stacked across several distinct programs that people often conflate:

- **Mutterschutz** — statutory protection/leave *before and immediately after* birth, for employees.
- **Mutterschaftsgeld** — the pay you receive *during* Mutterschutz.
- **Geburtsurkunde** — the birth certificate, the root document everything else depends on.
- **Kindergeld** — an ongoing monthly child benefit, paid regardless of income, for years.
- **Elterngeld** — a separate, time-limited income-replacement payment for the months around birth.
- **Elternzeit** — the *unpaid, job-protected leave* right that Elterngeld money is meant to support you through (they are not the same thing — you can take Elternzeit without claiming Elterngeld, and vice versa in theory, though in practice most people combine them).

All of these apply "regardless of nationality" as long as you're legally resident and (for most of them) legally employed/registered in Germany — but eligibility details for Kindergeld and Elterngeld depend heavily on your residence permit type.

## Step-by-step process

### 1. Before birth — Mutterschutz (maternity protection)

- **Protection period**: 6 weeks before your due date + at least 8 weeks after birth (14 weeks total minimum). For multiple births (twins etc.) or medically confirmed premature births, the post-birth period extends to 12 weeks (18 weeks total). Any days the baby arrives early are added onto the post-birth period rather than lost.
- **Who's covered**: All employed women, regardless of nationality, contract type (including fixed-term and mini-jobs), or length of service — there's no minimum employment period required. Civil servants (Beamtinnen) follow separate, state-specific rules. Self-employed/freelance women are **not automatically covered** by the Mutterschutzgesetz; whether you receive maternity pay depends on your specific statutory health insurance arrangement (verify with your Krankenkasse — voluntary GKV members can often opt into sickness-benefit-style coverage, PKV members generally cannot get an equivalent).
- **Working restrictions during pregnancy** (before the protection period kicks in): no night work (10pm–6am), no work between 8–10pm without written consent and approval, no Sunday/holiday work without written consent, no overtime, and physical restrictions (no lifting above ~5kg unassisted, no prolonged standing after month 6, no hazardous-substance exposure). Employers must do a workplace risk assessment and adjust/reassign duties if needed.
- **Notifying your employer**: notify in writing as soon as you know you're pregnant (there's no strict legal deadline to do so, but protections are strongest once your employer has been informed). Separately, notify your **Krankenversicherung (health insurer)** at least ~7 weeks before your due date and submit a doctor's/midwife's certificate confirming the due date — this triggers the Mutterschaftsgeld process.
- **Mutterschaftsgeld (maternity pay)**:
  - For GKV-insured employees: your statutory health insurer pays up to **€13/calendar day** (roughly €390/month), and your **employer is legally required to top up the difference** so your net income during Mutterschutz matches your average net daily wage from the prior 3 months. Net effect: you're paid your normal net salary throughout Mutterschutz, just split between two payers.
  - For unemployed/uninsured women: a one-time flat payment of **€210** from the Bundesamt für Soziale Sicherung (this figure has reportedly stayed unchanged for years — verify).
  - For women receiving unemployment benefits: the health insurer pays an amount matching your current unemployment benefit; no employer top-up applies (there's no employer).
  - Students with GKV coverage can also qualify for the €13/day benefit.
- **Dismissal protection**: employers are legally barred from dismissing you from the moment they learn of the pregnancy through the end of Mutterschutz — no exceptions carved out for contract type or nationality.

### 2. Giving birth & registering it

- ~98% of German births happen in hospitals; home births and Geburtshaus (birthing centre) options exist for low-risk pregnancies. All are covered under statutory (GKV) insurance with essentially zero out-of-pocket cost. Typical hospital stay: ~3 days for vaginal birth, 5–7 days for a Kaiserschnitt (C-section). You'll typically have a Hebamme (midwife) involved throughout pregnancy, birth, and postpartum care — arrange one early in pregnancy, as availability in and around Nuremberg can be tight.
- **Registering the birth (Geburtsurkunde)**: German law requires registering a birth at the **Standesamt (registry office)** within **1 week (7 days)** of birth. In Nuremberg this is the **Standesamt im Bürgeramt Mitte**. The hospital will usually hand you the paperwork/forms needed and may even help coordinate directly with the Standesamt for a birth registered there. An appointment (Termin) is generally required.
- **Documents needed to register the birth**:
  - [ ] Hospital birth confirmation (Geburtsanzeige/Geburtsbescheinigung from the hospital or midwife)
  - [ ] Both parents' passports/IDs
  - [ ] Meldebescheinigung / Anmeldebestätigung (proof of registered address)
  - [ ] Marriage certificate (Heiratsurkunde), if married
  - [ ] **Vaterschaftsanerkennung** (paternity acknowledgment), if unmarried — this can and should be prepared *before* the birth at the Standesamt or Jugendamt (youth welfare office), free of charge, so the father can be listed on the birth certificate without delay
- **Request multiple certified copies of the Geburtsurkunde at once** — you will need separate originals/certified copies for: Kindergeld application, Elterngeld application, adding the child to your health insurance, and (if applicable) passport applications for the child at your home country's consulate. Ordering 4–5 certified copies up front avoids repeat trips/fees later.

### 3. Kindergeld (child benefit)

- **What it is**: a flat, ongoing monthly cash benefit per child, independent of household income, paid until the child turns 18 (or up to 25 if in education/training/certain conditions).
- **Current amount**: **€259/month per child** as of January 2026 (raised from €255 — the German government has signalled further staged increases toward ~€272/month by 2028; verify current figure before relying on it).
- **Where to apply**: the **Familienkasse**, a division of the Bundesagentur für Arbeit (Federal Employment Agency). For Nuremberg, the relevant office is **Familienkasse Bayern Nord**, located at Solgerstraße 1, 90429 Nürnberg (postal address: Familienkasse Bayern-Nord, 90316 Nürnberg). Apply online via the Familienkasse portal, by post using form **KG1** (plus a **KG11** annex per child), or in person.
- **Documents needed**: original Geburtsurkunde (not a photocopy — submitting copies is reportedly the single most common rejection reason), valid passport/ID, Anmeldung confirmation, and — for non-EU nationals — proof of a qualifying residence permit (see below).
- **Eligibility for foreigners**: residence, not nationality, is the deciding factor. EU citizens with valid residence rights generally qualify automatically. Non-EU nationals generally need either a **Niederlassungserlaubnis (settlement permit)** or a temporary residence permit that permits work/residency — some temporary/precarious permit types may not qualify, so check your specific permit category against current Familienkasse rules.
- **Timeline & retroactive payment**: processing can take weeks to a few months; payments can be backdated up to **6 months** from application, but late filing beyond that window means that money is permanently lost — apply as soon as possible after birth.
- **Common problems**: submitting photocopies instead of originals; assuming Kindergeld stops automatically at 18 without notifying Familienkasse of ongoing education; failing to notify Familienkasse when leaving Germany (can trigger repayment demands); confusion among non-EU parents about which residence permit types qualify; only one parent per child can receive payments at a time, which occasionally causes disputes in separated/co-parenting situations.

### 4. Elterngeld (parental allowance)

- **What it is**: a separate, time-limited income-replacement benefit (distinct from Kindergeld) meant to offset lost income while a parent reduces work hours to care for a newborn.
- **Three variants**:
  - **Basiselterngeld**: replaces roughly **65–67% of prior average net income** (sources vary — verify current percentage), paid monthly between **€300 and €1,800/month**, for up to **12 months total** (shareable between both parents; a single parent or a couple where only one parent claims can generally use ~12 months, extending to 14 months if both parents each take at least 2 "Partnermonate" — partner bonus months — of leave).
  - **ElterngeldPlus**: stretches the same total money over roughly double the time at half the monthly rate (**€150–€900/month**) for up to **24 months**, useful for parents who want to work part-time (up to 32 hrs/week) while still drawing partial Elterngeld.
  - **Partnerschaftsbonus**: additional bonus months (**€150–€900/month**) available when both parents simultaneously reduce to part-time work for a defined period.
  - Income earned during the Elterngeld period reduces the payment amount, since it's calculated against your income drop.
  - **Income cap**: for births from April 2025 onward, couples/single parents with taxable annual income above **€175,000** are not eligible at all — verify this threshold for your birth date.
- **Where to apply in Bavaria**: the **ZBFS (Zentrum Bayern Familie und Soziales)** — specifically, for Nuremberg/Middle Franconia, the **Elterngeldstelle at ZBFS Region Mittelfranken**, Bärenschanzstraße 8c, 90429 Nürnberg. Phone: 0911 928 2444; email: poststelle.mfr@zbfs.bayern.de. Applications are submitted online via the ZBFS portal or by paper form.
- **Documents needed**: Geburtsurkunde, both parents' IDs, proof of income (payslips for employees covering the prior ~12 months; for freelancers, the prior calendar year's tax assessment/income proof), health insurance confirmation, and — if applicable — an estimate of expected income during the Elterngeld period.
- **Deadline / retroactive limit**: Elterngeld is only paid retroactively for a maximum of **3 months** before the application date — filing late means permanently losing those months' payments, so apply promptly after birth.
- **Common problems**:
  - **Freelancers/self-employed**: the calculation basis differs from employees — for employees it's the 12 months before birth; for the self-employed, it's the **previous full calendar year's** tax return, and the Elterngeldstelle also wants an estimate of expected income *during* the Elterngeld period, which is awkward for people with variable freelance income across multiple clients. This is one of the most-cited pain points on expat forums.
  - **Processing backlogs**: typical processing window is reported around 4–7 weeks, but a backlog, a missing document, or a complex income history can push this well past that without anything having actually gone wrong — a common recommendation is to budget for **2–3 months without Elterngeld income** as a buffer after birth.
  - Confusing Elterngeld (the money) with Elternzeit (the leave) — they're applied for separately, to different offices.

### 5. Elternzeit (parental leave — distinct from Elterngeld)

- **What it is**: a *legal right to unpaid leave* from your employer, separate from any Elterngeld payment. Each parent is entitled to **up to 36 months (3 years) of Elternzeit per child**, which can be split into up to three separate blocks; at least part of it must be used before the child's 3rd birthday, and the remainder can be saved and used any time up until the child turns 8.
- **Requesting it from your employer**: must be submitted **in writing** (a signed physical letter — email alone does not satisfy the legal notice requirement under German law) stating the exact intended start and end dates.
  - **Notice period**: at least **7 weeks** before the start, if taken within the child's first 3 years.
  - At least **13 weeks** notice if taken between the child's 3rd and 8th birthday.
  - When applying for Elternzeit within the first 3 years, you generally have to declare your intended leave plan for that period upfront.
- **Job protection**: dismissal is prohibited from **8 weeks before** the Elternzeit start date through the entire duration of the leave — protection begins as soon as you submit the formal written request, not just once leave starts. Very narrow exceptions exist (employer insolvency, serious breach) and require prior approval from the relevant occupational/labor authority.
- **Part-time work during Elternzeit**: you can work up to **32 hours/week** while on Elternzeit; if you were full-time before, you can request reduced hours from your employer. Income earned this way can reduce any concurrent Elterngeld payment (since Elterngeld compensates for lost income). Once Elternzeit ends, your original employment terms (hours, role) are restored automatically.
- Elternzeit itself pays nothing — it only guarantees the unpaid, job-protected time off; **Elterngeld** is the (separate) money that most parents use to cover that gap financially, and the two are usually — but not necessarily — timed to overlap.

## Documents / Requirements checklist

- [ ] Hospital/midwife birth confirmation (Geburtsbescheinigung)
- [ ] Both parents' passports/IDs
- [ ] Meldebescheinigung / Anmeldebestätigung (address registration)
- [ ] Marriage certificate (if married) or Vaterschaftsanerkennung (paternity acknowledgment, if unmarried — ideally prepared before birth)
- [ ] Multiple certified copies of the Geburtsurkunde (birth certificate) — request 4–5 up front
- [ ] Proof of residence permit type (non-EU nationals, for Kindergeld eligibility)
- [ ] KG1 form + KG11 annex (Kindergeld application)
- [ ] Payslips (last ~12 months) or prior year's tax assessment (self-employed) for Elterngeld
- [ ] Health insurance confirmation
- [ ] Written Elternzeit request letter to employer (signed, physical/postal — not just email), with exact dates
- [ ] Medical certificate confirming due date, submitted to your Krankenversicherung ~7 weeks before due date (for Mutterschaftsgeld)

## Costs (EUR, verify current figures)

Most of the money here flows **to** you rather than from you — this is one of the few pages in this wiki where "costs" mostly means benefits received:

- **Mutterschaftsgeld**: statutory health insurer pays up to ~€13/day (~€390/month); employer tops up the rest so your net pay is unchanged during Mutterschutz. Uninsured/unemployed: one-time €210 flat payment.
- **Kindergeld**: **€259/month per child** (as of Jan 2026), ongoing until 18 (or up to 25 in education) — likely to rise further in 2027/2028 per announced government plans (verify).
- **Elterngeld — Basiselterngeld**: €300–€1,800/month, up to 12 months (14 with partner bonus months), roughly 65–67% of prior net income (verify exact percentage).
- **Elterngeld — ElterngeldPlus**: €150–€900/month, up to 24 months.
- **Elterngeld — Partnerschaftsbonus**: €150–€900/month, additional bonus months when both parents work part-time simultaneously.
- **Vaterschaftsanerkennung**: free of charge at the Standesamt or Jugendamt.
- **Birth itself**: essentially €0 out-of-pocket under GKV (statutory) insurance, covering hospital delivery, midwife, and standard postnatal care.
- **Certified Geburtsurkunde copies**: modest per-copy fee (order several at once at the Standesamt — check current fee schedule).

## Timeline

- **~7 weeks before due date**: notify your Krankenversicherung with a medical certificate confirming the due date (triggers Mutterschaftsgeld).
- **6 weeks before due date → 8–12 weeks after birth**: Mutterschutz protection period.
- **Within 7 days of birth**: register the birth at the Standesamt and obtain the Geburtsurkunde.
- **As soon as possible after birth**: apply for Kindergeld (retroactive only up to 6 months) and Elterngeld (retroactive only up to 3 months) — both have hard backdating limits, so don't delay.
- **7 weeks before intended start (13 weeks if starting after child turns 3)**: submit written Elternzeit request to your employer.
- **Elterngeld processing**: typically ~4–7 weeks, but budget for 2–3 months of no income from this source as a buffer in case of backlogs or missing documents.

## Common problems & issues

- Submitting **photocopies instead of originals** for Kindergeld — the Familienkasse will reject and return the whole package.
- Missing the **6-month Kindergeld** or **3-month Elterngeld** retroactive-payment deadlines and permanently losing that money.
- Non-EU parents unsure whether their specific residence permit type qualifies for Kindergeld/Elterngeld — worth confirming directly with Familienkasse/ZBFS before assuming eligibility either way.
- Freelancers/self-employed facing a materially more complex Elterngeld calculation (prior calendar year income + estimated future income during leave) than employees.
- Processing backlogs at both Familienkasse and the Elterngeldstelle causing real financial gaps around the time of birth — plan a cash buffer.
- Confusing **Elterngeld** (money) with **Elternzeit** (unpaid job-protected leave) — they are applied for separately, to different offices, with different deadlines.
- Self-employed people are not automatically covered by Mutterschutz protections and need to check their own health insurer's rules for equivalent maternity pay.
- Unmarried fathers who don't arrange the Vaterschaftsanerkennung before birth can face delays getting listed on the birth certificate and, downstream, on Kindergeld/Elterngeld paperwork.

## Benefits

- Extensive, largely automatic legal protections during pregnancy and shortly after birth (job security, restricted working conditions, dismissal ban) apply regardless of nationality or contract type.
- Mutterschaftsgeld effectively preserves your full net income during Mutterschutz, split between insurer and employer.
- Kindergeld is a long-running, no-income-test benefit that continues for years, providing predictable ongoing support.
- Elterngeld offers meaningful income replacement (up to €1,800/month) during the early months of parenting, with flexible variants (Basis/Plus/Partnerschaftsbonus) to match different work arrangements.
- Elternzeit gives a strong, multi-year, job-protected right to reduce or pause work without needing employer permission (just proper notice) and without losing your position.
- GKV-covered childbirth is essentially free out-of-pocket.

## Drawbacks

- Multiple overlapping programs (Mutterschutz/Mutterschaftsgeld/Kindergeld/Elterngeld/Elternzeit), each with its own office, application, and deadline — easy to miss a retroactive-payment window.
- Self-employed/freelance parents get meaningfully less automatic protection (Mutterschutz) and a more complex application process (Elterngeld) than employees.
- Elternzeit itself is unpaid — without also securing Elterngeld (or savings), the leave right alone doesn't cover living costs.
- Processing delays at Familienkasse/ZBFS are common enough that financial planning around birth should assume a buffer, not the stated processing time.
- Foreign nationals on certain residence permit types may face genuine uncertainty (not just paperwork friction) about Kindergeld/Elterngeld eligibility, worth resolving before counting on the income.

## Nuremberg-specific tips

- **Standesamt im Bürgeramt Mitte** (birth registration/Geburtsurkunde): Hirschelgasse 32, 90403 Nürnberg — appointment (Termin) generally required; verify current address/hours before visiting, as some sources also list the broader Bürgeramt Mitte at Äußere Laufer Gasse 25.
- **Familienkasse Bayern Nord** (Kindergeld, covers Nuremberg): Solgerstraße 1, 90429 Nürnberg; postal address Familienkasse Bayern-Nord, 90316 Nürnberg; phone 0800 4 5555 30. Online/postal application is encouraged over in-person visits.
- **ZBFS Region Mittelfranken — Elterngeldstelle** (Elterngeld, covers Nuremberg): Bärenschanzstraße 8c, 90429 Nürnberg; phone 0911 928 2444; email poststelle.mfr@zbfs.bayern.de. Use the ZBFS Zuständigkeitsfinder online tool to confirm you have the right regional office if you're outside city limits.
- Book Hebamme (midwife) support early — availability around Nuremberg can be tight, especially for postpartum home visits.
- Order multiple certified Geburtsurkunde copies at the Standesamt appointment itself rather than making a second trip — you'll need separate copies for Kindergeld, Elterngeld, health insurance, and possibly a home-country passport application for the child.
