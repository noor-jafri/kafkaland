# Ausländerbehörde & Residence Permit (Aufenthaltstitel) — Nuremberg

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Main quest #7 — the big boss fight for non-EU visa holders (EU citizens can mostly skip).
**Prerequisites:** [04-city-registration-anmeldung.md](04-city-registration-anmeldung.md), [06-health-insurance.md](06-health-insurance.md).
**Unlocks:** Legal long-term residency; enables [08-integration-course.md](08-integration-course.md) obligations and [09-driving-license.md](09-driving-license.md) planning.

## Overview

The **Ausländerbehörde** (foreigners' registration office) is the local authority that decides whether a non-EU citizen may live and work in Germany. In Nuremberg it operates under the name **Amt für Migration und Integration** (Office for Migration and Integration), part of the city administration.

What it does:
- Issues, extends, and cancels residence titles (**Aufenthaltstitel**) — including the Aufenthaltserlaubnis (temporary residence permit), EU Blue Card, student residence permit, family reunification permit, freelance/self-employment permit, and eventually the Niederlassungserlaubnis (permanent settlement permit).
- Issues the **Fiktionsbescheinigung** — a bridge document confirming your legal status while an application/extension is being processed.
- Handles the biometric **eAT** (elektronischer Aufenthaltstitel, the credit-card-sized residence permit card) appointment.
- Processes naturalization (Einbürgerung) applications and provides migration counseling referrals.
- Handles lost-document reports and address/status changes for non-EU nationals.

**EU citizens**: EU/EEA/Swiss citizens do **not** need a residence permit to live or work in Germany — they only need to register their address (Anmeldung) at the Bürgeramt. This entire file does not apply to them, though they may still visit the Ausländerbehörde voluntarily for a registration certificate (Anmeldebescheinigung) or if applying for citizenship.

## Step-by-step process

1. **Register your address (Anmeldung)** at the Bürgeramt first — you cannot book most Ausländerbehörde appointments without a valid Anmeldebestätigung.
2. **Submit your application online first.** As of early 2025, Nuremberg moved most residence-permit services to a new online-first system (no longer processed via the old "Mein Nürnberg" portal, except letters of commitment/Verpflichtungserklärung). You fill in an online form and upload required documents.
3. **Wait for an invitation letter by post.** After reviewing your online submission, the office mails you an appointment (Termin) with date, time, and the specific one of its three locations you must attend. Since a nationwide digital visa portal rollout (from Feb 2026), some Ausländerbehörden also allow document submission and fee payment online before the in-person appointment — verify whether Nuremberg supports this yet.
4. **Attend the in-person appointment** with all original documents plus copies. Bring proof of purpose (employment contract, university enrollment, marriage certificate, etc.), proof of health insurance, proof of address, and financial means.
5. **Biometric data capture (eAT).** A biometric passport photo and two index-fingerprint scans are taken. From May 1, 2025, biometric photos can only be submitted digitally — either via a self-service machine at the office or a certified photographer who uploads the photo to a secure cloud with a QR code you bring to the appointment.
6. **Receive a Fiktionsbescheinigung** if your card isn't ready or a decision is pending — this legally extends your prior status (right to work, right to travel) under §81 AufenthG while you wait.
7. **Card production.** The physical eAT card is not printed locally — it is centrally manufactured by the Bundesdruckerei (Federal Printing Office) in Berlin. Typical turnaround is **4–6 weeks** from the appointment date.
8. **Pick up / receive the card** — usually mailed or collected in person depending on office practice.
9. **Renew before expiry.** Start the renewal process 4–8 weeks before your current permit expires to avoid a gap; you can typically apply for extension via the same online-first process.

## Documents/Requirements checklist

General documents needed for most Aufenthaltstitel applications:
- Valid passport
- Biometric passport photo (digital submission required since May 2025)
- Completed application form
- Proof of registered address (Anmeldebestätigung)
- Proof of health insurance (statutory or private, valid for Germany)
- Proof of financial means (varies by permit type)
- Current residence permit / visa (if extending or converting)

By visa/permit type:

| Permit type | Extra documents typically required |
|---|---|
| **Work visa / general employment permit** | Signed employment contract, job description, sometimes Bundesagentur für Arbeit approval |
| **EU Blue Card** | University degree (recognized/equivalent), employment contract meeting salary threshold (verify current figures — commonly cited around €45,000–€51,000/year depending on occupation and shortage-occupation status) |
| **Job seeker visa (holders converting in-country)** | Proof of qualification, proof of funds (~€1,000+/month, verify current figure), proof of accommodation |
| **Student residence permit** | University enrollment/admission letter, blocked account or scholarship proof of funds (verify current annual threshold, commonly cited near €11,000–€12,000/year), health insurance for students |
| **Family reunification (spouse/partner)** | Legalised marriage certificate with certified German translation, A1 German certificate (with exceptions for Blue Card spouses/spouses of German citizens), proof of sponsoring partner's income and adequate housing |
| **Freelance visa (Freiberufler)** | Business plan/proof of viable freelance activity, proof of qualification, letters of intent from clients, proof of funds, health insurance |
| **Niederlassungserlaubnis (permanent settlement)** | 5 years continuous legal residence (or less for Blue Card/skilled workers — as little as 21 months with B1 German for Blue Card holders), B1 German certificate, Rentenversicherungsverlauf (pension contribution record from Deutsche Rentenversicherung — request 6–8 weeks ahead), 3–6 months of recent payslips, adequate housing (roughly 9 m² per adult, 6 m² per child cited as a rule of thumb — verify), no reliance on social welfare |

All foreign-issued documents generally need certified/sworn German translations, and some need an apostille or legalisation.

## Costs (EUR, verify current figures)

- Initial issuance or extension of a temporary Aufenthaltserlaubnis: roughly **€93–€100** (some sources cite up to €150 depending on permit type)
- Initial eAT card issuance: around **€100–€110**
- eAT renewal: around **€93**
- Family/dependent permits: around **€100** each (verify — often lower for minors)
- Niederlassungserlaubnis: fee is separate and generally higher than a standard extension (verify current amount with Nuremberg office)
- These are official government fees only — translation, apostille, blocked account setup, and health insurance costs are separate and can add several hundred euros.

## Timeline

- Appointment booking: online submission first, then wait for a mailed invitation — **can take several weeks to months** depending on office backlog. Book/apply as early as possible; earlier applicants get noticeably better slot availability.
- Card production after appointment: **4–6 weeks** (centralized Bundesdruckerei production).
- Fiktionsbescheinigung bridges any gap between old permit expiry and new permit/card issuance — apply for renewal at least 4–8 weeks before expiry to ensure a Fiktionsbescheinigung can be issued in time if needed.
- Niederlassungserlaubnis eligibility: 5 years standard; as short as 21 months for EU Blue Card holders with B1 German.

## Common problems & issues

- **Long appointment wait times.** Multiple sources note appointment allocation "can take several weeks or even months" system-wide, and general survey data shows the Ausländerbehörde is consistently rated among the most frustrating German government offices to deal with (one survey found 54% of respondents named it the most problematic office).
- **System transition confusion.** Nuremberg's 2025 shift away from the "Mein Nürnberg" portal to a new online-first process reportedly caused confusion for applicants used to the old system.
- **Conflicting information from staff** — a commonly reported issue across German Ausländerbehörden generally (not unique to Nuremberg) where different caseworkers give different document requirements.
- **Last-minute document requests** — applicants report being asked for additional paperwork (translations, updated payslips, insurance proof) only at the appointment itself, causing delays or a need for a follow-up appointment.
- **Fear of visa gap** — if a permit expires before the renewal appointment or decision comes through, the Fiktionsbescheinigung is the safety net, but timing it correctly (applying early enough) is a frequently cited source of anxiety in expat forums.
- **Booking system quirks** — anecdotal tips from expat guides suggest trying to book early morning or late evening when the online portal has less traffic, and clearing browser cache if the booking page fails to load.
- **Three separate office locations** in Nuremberg (Äußere Laufer Gasse 25, Hirschelgasse 32, Regensburger Str. 231) — which location you're assigned depends on your case type/district, which can be confusing for newcomers who assume there's one central office.
- **When the office simply won't respond:** German administrative courts do not accept staff shortages or high application volume as a valid excuse for excessive delay. If you've sent a written status request with a firm deadline and heard nothing, an **Untätigkeitsklage** (action for failure to act, §75 VwGO) — a lawsuit compelling the authority to decide — is a recognised legal remedy, though it usually requires a lawyer. InterNations' *Expat Insider 2025* survey ranked Germany 46th of 46 countries on "Expat Essentials" (bureaucracy, housing, digital life, language), with 65% of expats rating German bureaucracy negatively — the Ausländerbehörde is consistently cited as the sharpest edge of that experience.
- **Emergency appointments** are possible by phone for urgent cases, but availability is inconsistent.

## Benefits

- A valid Aufenthaltstitel is the legal foundation for working, studying, opening bank accounts, and general life stability in Germany.
- The Fiktionsbescheinigung protects your right to work and travel while an application is pending, preventing an unintentional lapse into illegal status.
- Several visa types (EU Blue Card, skilled workers, graduates, self-employed) have accelerated paths to permanent residency.
- Once granted, the Niederlassungserlaubnis removes the need for any further renewals or Ausländerbehörde visits for status purposes.

## Drawbacks

- Bureaucratic, paper-heavy process with long wait times that can create genuine stress around visa gaps, job start dates, and travel plans.
- Requires original documents plus certified German translations for anything foreign-issued — costly and time-consuming to arrange.
- The card itself is centrally produced, so even after a successful appointment there's a multi-week wait with no card in hand (the Fiktionsbescheinigung substitutes but isn't as universally recognized by third parties, e.g. some banks or landlords).
- Recurring fees on every extension.
- Office capacity constraints in a growing city like Nuremberg mean appointment scarcity is a structural, not just occasional, problem.

## Nuremberg-specific tips / office info

- **Official name:** Amt für Migration und Integration (functions as the Ausländerbehörde for Nuremberg).
- **Website:** nuernberg.de/internet/auslaenderbehoerde/
- **Phone (general inquiries):** 0911 231-0 — Mon–Thu 7:30–17:00, Fri 7:30–15:30
- **Phone (urgent matters):** 0911 231-4700 — Mon–Fri 8:30–12:30
- **Locations:**
  - Äußere Laufer Gasse 25
  - Hirschelgasse 32
  - Regensburger Str. 231
- **Booking process:** Submit your request online first via the city's system; you'll then be mailed an appointment letter specifying which of the three locations to go to. There's no walk-in booking without this online first step, except emergencies handled by phone.
- **No pets allowed** in the office buildings except registered assistance animals.
- **Rescheduling** existing appointments is possible online with a stated reason.
- Apply for extensions/renewals well before your permit's expiry date given Nuremberg's documented backlog — starting 2–3 months ahead is a commonly recommended buffer in expat guides for mid-size German cities.
