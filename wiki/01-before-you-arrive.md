# Before You Arrive: Visas, Documents & Preparation for Moving to Nuremberg

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Main quest #1.
**Prerequisites:** None — this is the starting point.
**Unlocks:** [02-arrival-and-first-days.md](02-arrival-and-first-days.md).

## Overview

Moving to Nuremberg (part of Bavaria, in the Nuremberg Metropolitan Region) requires different preparation depending on your citizenship and the reason for your move. This file covers the pre-arrival process for all common visa/entry routes:

- **EU/EEA/Swiss citizens** – no visa needed, freedom of movement applies
- **Work visa (skilled worker)** – job offer required, qualification recognition often required
- **EU Blue Card** – for university-degree holders with a qualifying salary offer
- **Job Seeker Visa / Opportunity Card (Chancenkarte)** – to search for work in Germany
- **Student visa** – for university admission, requires blocked account (Sperrkonto)
- **Family reunification / spouse visa** – to join a relative already in Germany
- **Freelance/self-employment visa (Freiberufler/Selbständige)** – for freelancers and business founders

Nuremberg-specific institutions you will interact with once you arrive are covered in `02-arrival-and-first-days.md`, but pre-arrival document prep is the same regardless of destination city.

## Step-by-step process

### 1. EU/EEA/Swiss citizens
1. Confirm you only need a valid passport or national ID card — no visa or residence permit is required.
2. Arrange health insurance (EHIC/GHIC covers short stays, but you'll need German statutory or private insurance for longer stays/work).
3. Start your housing search before arrival (see below) — you'll need an address to register (Anmeldung) within 14 days of moving in.
4. No embassy appointment needed; you can travel and register directly in Nuremberg.

### 2. Work visa (skilled worker)
1. Secure a written job offer/contract matching your qualifications.
2. Get your foreign qualification/degree recognized if required for your profession (regulated professions especially — check via [anerkennung-in-deutschland.de](https://www.anerkennung-in-deutschland.de)).
3. Gather documents (see checklist below).
4. Book an appointment at your local German consulate/embassy — do this as early as possible; backlogs are common.
5. Attend the appointment: submit application, provide biometrics, pay the €75 visa fee.
6. Wait for processing (weeks to months, varies heavily by country/mission).
7. Travel to Germany on the entry visa; convert it to a residence permit at the local Ausländerbehörde (foreigners' office) after registering your address.

### 3. EU Blue Card
1. Secure a job offer/contract with a minimum duration of 6 months meeting the salary threshold (2026 general threshold reported around €50,700/year gross; lower thresholds for shortage occupations like IT, reported around €45,900/year — verify current figures with your consulate or make-it-in-germany.com, as thresholds are adjusted periodically).
2. Confirm your academic degree is recognized in Germany (or equivalent professional experience for some shortage occupations).
3. Apply at the German mission in your country of residence.
4. Note: some nationalities (e.g., US, Canada, Australia, Japan, South Korea, Israel, UK, New Zealand) can enter visa-free and apply for the Blue Card directly at the local Ausländerbehörde after arrival — check eligibility for your nationality before skipping the embassy step.

### 4. Job Seeker Visa / Opportunity Card (Chancenkarte)
1. Confirm eligibility: recognized qualification (academic or vocational) and, for some routes, professional experience.
2. Prove financial resources to support yourself without a job (reported 2026 figures around €1,091/month — i.e., roughly €6,546 for 6 months or €13,092 for 12 months; verify current amounts).
3. Apply at least ~3 months before your planned entry, since processing can take 4–6 weeks and appointment slots are limited.
4. The classic Job Seeker Visa is valid 6 months (non-extendable) purely for job hunting; the Opportunity Card (Chancenkarte) is valid 1 year and extendable, and allows limited work/trial work while searching.
5. Once you secure a job in Germany, convert your permit at the local Ausländerbehörde.

### 5. Student visa
1. Get admission (Zulassungsbescheid) from a German university, or apply as a "university applicant" if not yet admitted.
2. Open a blocked account (Sperrkonto) and deposit the required annual amount (reported 2026 figure: €11,904/year, released at ~€992/month — verify current amount, as it is adjusted annually based on BAföG rates). Common providers: Expatrio, Fintiba, Deutsche Bank.
3. Arrange health insurance valid for visa purposes.
4. Gather proof of English or German language proficiency depending on program language.
5. Apply for the student visa at least 3 months before planned arrival; processing can take several weeks (some sources cite ~25 days, others longer depending on mission).
6. After arrival, register your address and convert to a residence permit at the local Ausländerbehörde.

### 6. Family reunification / spouse visa
1. Confirm the sponsoring partner in Germany is a German citizen, EU citizen, or holds a valid residence permit, and both partners are 18+.
2. Obtain the A1-level German language certificate (Goethe-Institut or telc) before applying — exceptions exist for highly skilled professionals, EU Blue Card holders, or spouses of German citizens in some cases.
3. Legalize the marriage certificate (apostille/legalization) and get certified German translations.
4. Gather proof of the sponsor's income and adequate housing space in Germany.
5. Apply at the German embassy/consulate in your home country — cannot be done from within Germany for most cases.
6. Processing commonly takes 3–6 months; incomplete documentation is a frequent cause of delay.
7. Convert the entry visa to a residence permit (Aufenthaltserlaubnis zur Familienzusammenführung) at the local Ausländerbehörde after arrival and Anmeldung.

### 7. Freelance/self-employment visa (Freiberufler / Selbständige)
1. Determine whether your profession qualifies as a "liberal profession" (Freiberufler — e.g., doctors, lawyers, architects, journalists, artists, IT consultants) or general self-employment (Gewerbe).
2. Prepare a business plan: services offered, target clients, projected income, and how the business benefits the German economy/region.
3. Gather proof of relevant qualifications, existing client contracts or letters of intent (especially useful for freelancers), and proof of funds to cover living costs.
4. Arrange health insurance (minimum €30,000 coverage for the visa application stage).
5. Apply at the German consulate in your home country, or in some cases directly at the local Ausländerbehörde if you're already a resident on another permit.
6. After arrival, register at the residents' registration office and report self-employment to the local tax office (Finanzamt); obtain a professional license if your field requires one.

## Documents/Requirements checklist

Bring originals plus certified copies for all visa types where applicable:

- Valid passport (with enough validity/blank pages)
- Biometric passport photos (per German visa photo specs)
- Completed visa application form
- Birth certificate — apostilled and with certified German translation if not in German/English
- Marriage certificate (if applicable) — apostilled + certified translation
- Degree/qualification certificates — apostilled + certified translation, plus recognition documentation (Anerkennung) if required for your profession
- Proof of health insurance (minimum €30,000 coverage for travel/visa-stage insurance; switch to German statutory/private insurance after arrival)
- Proof of financial means (job contract/salary proof, blocked account statement, or job-seeker financial proof depending on visa type)
- Proof of accommodation in Germany (rental contract, hotel booking, or invitation letter) — needed for the visa application itself in many cases
- CV/resume and cover letter (for work/job-seeker visas)
- A1 German language certificate (for spouse visa, unless exempted)
- Business plan and proof of qualifications (freelance visa)
- University admission letter/Zulassungsbescheid (student visa)
- Schufa credit report (sometimes requested for work visa/renting)
- Visa fee payment receipt (typically €75, standard national visa fee — verify current amount)

**Apostille/legalization note:** Germany is part of the Hague Apostille Convention. Public documents (birth certificates, marriage certificates, degrees) issued in Hague Convention countries need only an apostille from the relevant authority in the issuing country — not full embassy legalization. Non-Hague-Convention countries require consular legalization instead. Always pair the apostilled original with a certified German translation (2 copies is a commonly requested standard).

## Costs (EUR — verify current figures)

- National visa application fee: ~€75 (reduced or waived for some family reunification/minors cases — verify)
- Certified translations: roughly €30–80 per document depending on length and language
- Apostille/legalization: varies by country, often €10–50 per document plus courier costs
- Blocked account (Sperrkonto) deposit for students: reported ~€11,904/year (2026 figure, adjusted annually — verify with Fintiba/Expatrio/current embassy guidance)
- Job seeker financial proof: reported ~€6,546 (6 months) or ~€13,092 (12 months) — verify current BAföG-linked figures
- Travel/visa-stage health insurance: roughly €30–70/month depending on provider and coverage
- Courier/document shipping costs if applying by mail: €20–50

## Timeline / how long it takes

- **EU citizens:** no visa timeline — travel any time, register after arrival.
- **Work visa:** appointment booking can take weeks to months depending on your local mission's backlog; processing after submission commonly weeks to a few months.
- **EU Blue Card:** similar embassy timeline to work visa; visa-exempt nationals can skip the embassy step and apply directly at the Ausländerbehörde once in Germany, but Ausländerbehörde appointment backlogs (see below) can still add months.
- **Job Seeker Visa/Opportunity Card:** apply ~3 months ahead; processing ~4–6 weeks once appointment is secured.
- **Student visa:** apply at least 3 months before intended arrival; embassy processing reported as short as ~25 days in some cases, but can run longer.
- **Family reunification:** 3–6 months typical, longer if documents are incomplete or language certification is delayed.
- **Freelance visa:** timeline varies widely by consulate and case complexity; several weeks to a few months is common.
- **Note:** After any visa is granted and you arrive, converting to a full residence permit at the local Ausländerbehörde is a separate step with its own wait — see `02-arrival-and-first-days.md` and note the Nuremberg-specific backlog below.

## Common problems & issues people face

- **Embassy appointment backlogs:** Wait times for a first appointment at German missions abroad can range from a few weeks to several months depending on the country; some missions have suspended or restricted services entirely for periods due to surges in demand (e.g., regional crises).
- **Document rejection:** Incomplete documentation is consistently cited as the most common reason for delays or rejections — missing certified translations, missing apostilles, or expired documents are frequent culprits.
- **Language certificate delays:** For spouse visas, waiting for A1 German exam slots (Goethe-Institut/telc) can add weeks to the timeline.
- **Qualification recognition delays:** Non-EU degree holders sometimes face lengthy waits for the Anerkennung (recognition) process, which can hold up work/Blue Card visa applications.
- **Nuremberg's Ausländerbehörde (foreigners' office) backlog:** Once in Germany, converting your visa to a residence permit or renewing it at Nuremberg's immigration office has been reported by residents as extremely slow — some report waiting from initial application to card issuance for well over a year, with one case citing roughly 17 months and others citing 14 months just for a first appointment. The office attributes this to staff shortages (~15% of positions reportedly unfilled) and surging caseloads. Appointments can only be booked via the "Mein Nürnberg" online portal — walk-ins are generally turned away.
- **2026 policy change:** The remonstration (appeal) procedure for visa rejections has reportedly been abolished for missions worldwide as of mid-2026 — a rejected application may no longer have an appeal route, making it more important to get the application right the first time. Verify this with your specific consulate.
- **Post reports (forums/liveingermany.de):** Applicants commonly complain about unclear or missing communication from missions, no email confirmation of receipt, and inconsistent processing speed between consulates in different countries or even between German cities' immigration offices.

## Benefits and drawbacks by route

| Visa type | Benefits | Drawbacks |
|---|---|---|
| EU citizen | No visa needed, fastest route, full work rights immediately | N/A (not available to non-EU nationals) |
| Work visa | Clear path tied to a real job, can lead to permanent residency | Requires job offer first; qualification recognition can be slow |
| EU Blue Card | Fast track to permanent residency (reported 21–33 months), some nationalities can skip embassy | Salary threshold excludes many roles; requires recognized degree |
| Job Seeker/Opportunity Card | Lets you search for work while in Germany | No work rights (or only limited trial work) until a job is secured; must self-fund entire period |
| Student visa | Access to (often low-cost) German higher education, path to post-study work visa | Sperrkonto ties up significant capital; limited part-time work hours allowed |
| Family reunification | Full work rights from day one in many cases, family unity | A1 German requirement can delay timeline; long processing (3–6 months) |
| Freelance/self-employment | Independence, flexible income streams, appeals to liberal professions | Must prove sustainable income and market demand; harder for non-"liberal profession" freelancers; more paperwork with the Finanzamt |

## Nuremberg-specific tips

- The local foreigners' office (Ausländerbehörde) for Nuremberg is located at **Regensburger Str. 231, 90478 Nürnberg** (phone 0911/231-0). Appointments must be booked through the **"Mein Nürnberg"** online service portal — there is no walk-in service.
- Given the reported multi-month-to-multi-year backlog at Nuremberg's immigration office, apply for any appointment (residence permit conversion, extension) as early as your visa/status allows, and keep documentation of when you applied in case of delays affecting your legal stay status.
- Neighboring towns in the Nuremberg Metropolitan Region (e.g., Fürth, Erlangen) have reportedly processed cases faster than Nuremberg itself — this may be relevant if your residence address is flexible before you commit to a specific district.
- Housing search: start early using platforms like **ImmobilienScout24, Immonet, WG-Gesucht** (shared flats) for permanent housing, and **Wunderflats, HousingAnywhere, The Homelike** for furnished/temporary options — the Nuremberg rental market, especially near the city center and Technische Hochschule Nürnberg / Friedrich-Alexander-Universität Erlangen-Nürnberg, can be competitive.
