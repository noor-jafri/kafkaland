# SIM Card & Home Internet

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Side quest — mobile SIM is useful from hour one; home internet as soon as you have a lease.
**Prerequisites:** None for prepaid/eSIM. [04-city-registration-anmeldung.md](04-city-registration-anmeldung.md) + [05-bank-account.md](05-bank-account.md) for postpaid contracts.
**Unlocks:** A German phone number (needed for banking SMS codes, Anmeldung confirmations, delivery services) and a working connection at home.

## Overview

You'll need a working German number early — for banking SMS codes, Anmeldung appointment confirmations, delivery services, and everyday life. There are two fundamentally different paths for a phone plan: prepaid (Prepaid-SIM) and postpaid contract (Laufzeitvertrag/Vertrag). Separately, setting up home internet is one of the most frequently complained-about admin tasks in Germany — not because it's expensive, but because of long contract terms and slow installation.

## Process

### Mobile SIM
- **Prepaid:** Buy a SIM at a supermarket (Aldi Talk, Lidl Connect), drugstore, or provider shop, top up online or in-store, and activate — no Schufa (credit check) or German bank account required. This is the easiest option before you're fully settled.
- **Postpaid:** Requires a German bank account (for SEPA direct debit) and usually a clean Schufa score; contracts commonly run 24 months with auto-renewal unless cancelled in time. Providers include Deutsche Telekom (best rural/network coverage), Vodafone, and O2 (Telefónica), plus MVNOs like congstar, Lidl Connect, Aldi Talk, and Otelo that resell capacity from the big three at lower prices.
- **eSIM for the arrival gap:** Many newcomers buy a travel eSIM (Airalo, Holafly, or an eSIM from O2/Vodafone) valid before landing, so they have data and a way to receive verification codes during the first days before Anmeldung and a bank account are sorted.

### Home internet
- Main providers: Deutsche Telekom (~40% market share, best coverage), Vodafone (~30%, cable-based in many areas), O2/Telefónica, and resellers like 1&1.
- You sign a contract (often DSL, cable, or increasingly fiber/Glasfaser depending on the building), which then requires a technician appointment to activate the line at the street cabinet or in the building.
- If you're only staying short-term, ask specifically for a contract "ohne Mindestlaufzeit" (no minimum term) — Telekom, 1&1, and O2 all offer these, usually at a slightly higher monthly price.

## Costs (verify current figures)

- Prepaid SIM + starter data package: roughly €10–20/month depending on data allowance.
- Postpaid contracts: roughly €10–40/month depending on data and provider (major networks cost more than MVNOs for similar data).
- eSIM travel packages: a few euros to ~€20 depending on data volume and duration.
- Home internet: roughly €30–50/month for standard DSL/cable speeds, more for fiber. One-off setup/activation or router fees sometimes apply, occasionally waived as a sign-up promo.

## Common problems & issues

- Postpaid contracts require Anmeldung + bank account + often Schufa — a chicken-and-egg problem for people who land without either yet.
- Auto-renewing contracts are notoriously hard to cancel in time; missing the cancellation window locks you in for another term (though 2022 legislation shortened auto-renewal lock-in periods for new contracts — verify how this applies to your provider).
- Coverage varies significantly outside cities; Telekom generally has the best rural coverage in Bavaria.
- **Installation wait times of 2–6 weeks** between signing and actually getting a working home internet connection are common and widely reported as one of the most frustrating parts of moving to Germany — plan to rely on mobile hotspot/eSIM data in the meantime.
- Standard home internet contracts lock you in for **24 months**, and cancellation windows are strict; missing them silently renews the contract.
- Moving mid-contract can mean either transferring the contract to the new address (with its own wait) or paying to break it.

## Benefits

- Prepaid/eSIM options mean you can be connected within minutes of landing, with zero bureaucratic prerequisites.
- MVNOs offer major-network coverage at a fraction of the price for people willing to shop around.
- "Ohne Mindestlaufzeit" contracts exist for both mobile and internet if you know to ask, avoiding long lock-ins.

## Drawbacks

- Postpaid mobile and home internet both gate behind Anmeldung/bank account/Schufa — exactly the things you don't have on arrival.
- Home internet installation delays are one of the most consistently frustrating parts of settling in, often taking weeks.
- Long minimum contract terms (24 months) are the default unless you specifically negotiate otherwise.

## Tips

- Start with prepaid or an eSIM immediately on arrival, then switch to postpaid once Anmeldung and a bank account are done, if you want a better long-term rate.
- Order internet the moment you have a signed lease and address — don't wait until moving day.
- Ask your landlord/previous tenant if a line is already active in the flat; sometimes you can take over an existing contract (Vertragsübernahme) and skip the installation wait entirely.
- Check contract end/cancellation dates in a calendar reminder the day you sign, for both mobile and internet.
