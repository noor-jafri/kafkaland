## Overview

Health insurance is **mandatory** for everyone resident in Germany, and proof of valid coverage is required for almost every visa/residence-permit and Anmeldung-adjacent step. There are two systems:

- **GKV — gesetzliche Krankenversicherung (public/statutory insurance)**: income-based contributions, no health questionnaire, covers non-earning spouse/children for free (family co-insurance). Major providers: **TK (Techniker Krankenkasse)**, **AOK**, **Barmer**, DAK.
- **PKV — private Krankenversicherung (private insurance)**: premiums based on age/health at entry, cheaper when young and healthy but rises with age; each family member needs a separate (paid) policy.

Roughly 73 million people in Germany use GKV vs. ~8.7 million on PKV (order-of-magnitude figures — verify current stats).

### Who must use which

- **Employees** earning below the annual income threshold (reported around **€73,800–€77,400 gross/year for 2026**, figures vary by source — verify current BAMF/BMG figure) are generally **required** to use public insurance (GKV), automatically deducted from payroll.
- **Employees above the threshold**, **civil servants (Beamte)**, and **self-employed/freelancers** may choose private insurance (PKV) instead.
- **Students** (under 30, enrolled at a German university) get discounted statutory *student* rates via GKV, or can opt for private student insurance in some cases — but many providers/universities require public unless there's a specific exemption.
- **Freelancers/self-employed** generally choose voluntarily between GKV (income-based, roughly €200–950/month depending on income) or PKV (roughly €300–600/month for a young healthy person) — verify current figures. **Independent artists and publicists** may instead qualify for the **Künstlersozialkasse (KSK)**, which subsidizes ~half of health, long-term-care, and pension contributions like an employer would (minimum annual freelance income around €3,900 to qualify — verify).
- **Job seekers / visa applicants not yet employed**: need **incoming/travel health insurance** to cover the gap between arrival and starting work/study, then switch to full GKV/PKV once employed or enrolled.

## Step-by-step process

1. **Before the visa appointment**: obtain proof of **incoming health insurance** (travel-style coverage valid from arrival date) — required to upload with the visa application in the Consular Services Portal. Some providers (e.g., TK bundled with DR-WALTER) offer up to ~31 days of free incoming private insurance while your long-term plan activates.
2. **Choose long-term insurer**: For most employees/students, pick a public provider — TK, AOK Bayern, or Barmer are common recommendations for expats due to English-language support (TK is frequently cited as strongest for English service).
3. **Sign up** — online, by phone, or in person at a local Geschäftsstelle (branch office). Students must typically register within the **first 3 months** of enrollment.
4. **Provide employment contract or university enrollment (Immatrikulationsbescheinigung)** so the insurer can issue the correct Mitgliedsbescheinigung (membership confirmation) — this document is required for Anmeldung and residence-permit appointments.
5. **Employees**: contributions are deducted automatically from payroll once GKV membership starts; employer and employee split most of the ~14.6% base rate plus a Zusatzbeitrag (supplemental rate, varies by insurer, roughly 1.7–3.3%).
6. **Freelancers/self-employed**: pay contributions directly to the Krankenkasse (GKV) or insurer (PKV) monthly.
7. **Family members**: for GKV, register non-earning spouse/children for free family co-insurance (Familienversicherung) — income limits apply for the co-insured spouse.

## Documents / Requirements checklist

- [ ] Passport / ID
- [ ] Visa or residence permit (or proof of application in progress)
- [ ] Employment contract, or university enrollment/admission letter, or freelance registration (Gewerbeanmeldung / Finanzamt freelance registration)
- [ ] Proof of incoming/travel insurance for the pre-arrival/pre-enrollment gap
- [ ] Anmeldung confirmation (address) — some insurers request this to finalize registration
- [ ] Bank account (IBAN) for direct-debit contributions (self-employed/freelancers)
- [ ] For students: age (under 30) and full-time enrollment status to qualify for discounted student GKV rate
- [ ] For KSK: proof of self-employed artistic/publicist activity and minimum income threshold

## Costs (EUR — verify current figures)

- **Public (GKV) general rate**: contributions roughly **14.6% of gross income** (split employer/employee) **plus** an insurer-specific Zusatzbeitrag of about **1.7–3.3%** depending on provider (e.g., TK ~2.69% vs. Barmer ~3.29% — verify current rates).
- **Student GKV rates** (reported, verify current figures):
  - TK: ~€141.16/month (under 23)
  - AOK Bayern: ~€141.16/month (under 23)
  - Barmer: ~€146.29/month (under 23), rising to ~€151.42/month for 23+ without children (extra nursing-care surcharge)
- **Voluntary GKV for freelancers**: roughly €200–950/month, income-based.
- **Private (PKV)**: roughly €300–600/month for a young, healthy freelancer; increases substantially with age due to aging-reserve premium structure. Student private plans often start much lower (double-digit euros/month) but check what's actually covered.
- **Income threshold for choosing PKV as an employee**: reported around **€73,800 (or €77,400 per some sources) gross/year for 2026** — figures are inconsistent across sources, verify the exact current Versicherungspflichtgrenze.

## Timeline

- Incoming/travel insurance: purchase before visa appointment, valid from planned arrival date.
- Long-term GKV/PKV sign-up: ideally within days of starting a job or within the first 3 months of university enrollment (students must not exceed this window).
- Mitgliedsbescheinigung issuance: typically a few days to two weeks after registration — needed before/at the residence-permit appointment, so apply early.
- Family co-insurance registration: can be done alongside your own sign-up or shortly after.

## Common problems & issues

- **Private insurance used to satisfy visa requirements, then problems later**: some visa applicants take out cheap private insurance just to meet the visa checklist, not realizing PKV becomes very expensive with age and that switching back to GKV after roughly age 55 is "effectively impossible" under current German law (§5 SGB V narrows eligibility for returning to public insurance as people age). This is one of the most repeated warnings on expat forums.
- **Freelancers who opt out of GKV into PKV**: the decision to leave GKV as a self-employed person is generally **irrevocable** unless you later become an employee — a commonly cited trap.
- **Family co-insurance confusion**: PKV does not offer free family coverage — each family member (including children) needs an individual paid policy, which can multiply costs 2–3x versus a GKV household; people underestimate this when picking PKV.
- **Being incorrectly assessed into the wrong system**: e.g., students over 30 being pushed into more expensive options once they age out of discounted student GKV rates ("age-30 trap") — verify your specific status before it changes.
- **Insurer administrative delays** in issuing the Mitgliedsbescheinigung causing residence-permit appointment issues — apply for insurance as early as possible, well before the Ausländerbehörde appointment.
- **Language barriers** at branch offices — TK is frequently recommended over AOK/Barmer specifically for stronger English-language phone/app support, though AOK has more physical branches.
- **Provider/product bundling confusion**: incoming insurance (e.g., DR-WALTER via TK) is a separate, temporary product from the long-term GKV plan — some people mistakenly assume it auto-converts without action.

## Benefits

**Public (GKV)**
- No health questionnaire or exclusions for pre-existing conditions.
- Free family co-insurance for non-earning spouse/children.
- Costs scale with income (progressive fairness), predictable.
- Widely accepted, no re-underwriting risk as you age.

**Private (PKV)**
- Can be cheaper when young and healthy.
- Often faster appointment access and more service options.
- Some employer-paid top-up/PKV options for high earners and civil servants.

## Drawbacks

**Public (GKV)**
- Slower average appointment access reported vs. private.
- Contribution rate rises with income (no cap advantage for high earners below the threshold).

**Private (PKV)**
- Premiums rise substantially with age (aging-reserve model).
- No free family co-insurance — each member pays separately.
- Switching back to GKV becomes very difficult/impossible after around age 55.
- Health questionnaire at entry — pre-existing conditions can mean exclusions, loadings, or denial.
- For self-employed people, opting into PKV is generally irrevocable while staying self-employed.

## Nuremberg-specific tips

- **TK (Techniker Krankenkasse) Nürnberg**: branch at Südwestpark 65, 90449 Nürnberg (verify current address/hours before visiting).
- **AOK Bayern Nürnberg**: branch at Frauentorgraben 49, 90443 Nürnberg.
- **Barmer Nürnberg**: branch at Regensburger Str. 223, 90478 Nürnberg (reported hours: Mon 09:00–17:00, Tue–Wed 09:00–13:00, Thu 09:00–18:00, Fri 09:00–13:00 — verify before visiting).
- Nuremberg's Ausländerbehörde and university international offices (e.g., FAU/TH Nürnberg) generally expect the Mitgliedsbescheinigung in hand at the appointment — bring a printed copy in addition to any digital confirmation.
- Branch offices in and around Nuremberg (also nearby Fürth) can get busy; calling ahead or booking online where possible avoids long walk-in waits.
