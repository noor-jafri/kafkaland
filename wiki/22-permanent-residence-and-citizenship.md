# Permanent Residence & Citizenship

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Side quest — the long-game "endgame" for anyone planning to stay years, not months.
**Prerequisites:** [07-auslanderbehorde-residence-permit.md](07-auslanderbehorde-residence-permit.md) as an ongoing residence permit holder, [15-pension-insurance.md](15-pension-insurance.md) (pension contributions count toward qualifying time), working German (level requirement varies by route).
**Unlocks:** Security of status — the end of the permit-renewal treadmill, and (for citizenship) an EU passport and voting rights.

## Overview

Once you're settled and working, the long game is security of status. There are two milestones: **permanent residence** (Niederlassungserlaubnis — indefinite right to stay) and **naturalisation** (German citizenship). Citizenship rules were liberalised in 2024 and then partly reversed in 2025, so this is a genuinely moving target — verify current rules before planning around specific timelines.

## Step-by-step process

### Permanent residence (Niederlassungserlaubnis)

An indefinite residence permit — you no longer renew, and job-change restrictions ease.

| Route | Time to qualify | Language | Notes |
|-------|-----------------|----------|-------|
| EU Blue Card holder | 27 months (or 21 months with B1) | A1 (27mo) / B1 (21mo) | Fastest route; pension contributions throughout |
| Skilled worker (§18a/b) | 3 years | B1 | 36 months of pension contributions |
| Skilled worker with German degree | 2 years | B1 | Fastest non-Blue-Card route |
| General standard route (§9) | 5 years | B1 | 60 months pension + adequate housing, no reliance on Bürgergeld |

Common requirements across routes: secure livelihood, pension contributions, adequate housing, no serious criminal record, and usually B1 German (A1 for the fast Blue Card track).

### Citizenship (naturalisation)

1. Track your qualifying residence time under whichever route applies (see below).
2. Reach the required German level — B1 for the standard route.
3. Pass the "Leben in Deutschland" / naturalisation test (see [08-integration-course.md](08-integration-course.md) for the related Orientierungskurs test, which can overlap).
4. Demonstrate secure livelihood without reliance on state benefits, and a clean criminal record.
5. Apply through your local naturalisation authority (in Nuremberg, part of the Amt für Migration und Integration alongside the Ausländerbehörde functions).

## Documents/Requirements checklist

- Proof of qualifying residence duration (permit history)
- Proof of pension contributions (Rentenversicherung records — see [15-pension-insurance.md](15-pension-insurance.md))
- German language certificate at the required level
- "Leben in Deutschland" test certificate
- Proof of secure livelihood (employment contract/payslips) and adequate housing
- Clean criminal record certificate (Führungszeugnis)

## Costs (EUR, verify current figures)

- Naturalisation application fees are a modest flat administrative fee (verify current figure with your local naturalisation authority) — much smaller than most other steps in this wiki, but the time investment (years of residence) is the real cost.

## Timeline — the moving citizenship target

The rules were liberalised in 2024 and then partly reversed in 2025:

**The 2024 reform (in force 27 June 2024):**
- Dual citizenship generally permitted for the first time — you no longer have to give up your original nationality.
- Standard naturalisation residence requirement cut from 8 to 5 years.
- A 3-year fast track was introduced for exceptional integration (C1 German plus special achievements).

**The 2025 reversal (in force 30 October 2025):**
- Following the 2025 change of government, the Bundestag abolished the 3-year fast track on 8 October 2025.
- A uniform 5-year minimum was restored.
- Dual citizenship and the 5-year path remain.
- The reversal even affected pending applications filed under the 3-year rule — some were put on hold or rejected.

**Typical citizenship requirements (current 5-year route):**
- 5 years' lawful residence
- B1 German (higher for any special routes)
- "Leben in Deutschland" / naturalisation test passed
- Secure livelihood without reliance on state benefits
- Commitment to the free democratic constitutional order
- Clean criminal record

## Common problems & issues

- **Rules change fast, even mid-application.** The 2025 abolition of the 3-year fast track affected people who had already applied under it — some applications were put on hold or rejected outright. Track official updates continuously if you're on a multi-year path.
- **Pension contribution gaps** (job changes, periods of unemployment, freelance gaps) can quietly extend your qualifying timeline for permanent residence without you noticing until you check.
- **Language level underestimated** — B1 is a real bar for many applicants, and higher levels are required for fast-track routes.
- **Confusing permanent residence with citizenship** — they are separate milestones with separate requirements; reaching one doesn't automatically confer the other.

## Benefits

- Permanent residence removes the renewal treadmill and permit-tied-to-employer anxiety entirely.
- Citizenship adds voting rights, an EU passport, freedom of movement across the EU, and full security of status independent of any future visa/permit policy changes.
- Since 2024, dual citizenship is generally permitted, so most applicants no longer have to renounce their original nationality to naturalise.

## Drawbacks

- Germany's naturalisation rate has historically been below the EU average, with 12+ million residents lacking citizenship — the system is neither fast nor simple even after the 2024 reform.
- The 2025 partial reversal is a concrete example of how far along you can be in a process before rules shift under you.
- Multi-year qualifying periods make this the longest-horizon item in the entire wiki — plan around years, not appointments.

## Nuremberg-specific tips

- Naturalisation applications in Nuremberg are handled by the city's **Amt für Migration und Integration** — the same broader office family that runs the Ausländerbehörde functions covered in [07-auslanderbehorde-residence-permit.md](07-auslanderbehorde-residence-permit.md); check nuernberg.de for the current dedicated naturalisation contact point and required appointment process.
- Given the 2024/2025 rule volatility, confirm current requirements directly with the city office or a specialised immigration lawyer before submitting, rather than relying on general guides (including this one) for exact current timelines.
