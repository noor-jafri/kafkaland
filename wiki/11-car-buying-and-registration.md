# Car Buying and Registration in Nuremberg

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Side quest — optional, unlocked once you have a driving license valid in Germany.
**Prerequisites:** [09-driving-license.md](09-driving-license.md), [04-city-registration-anmeldung.md](04-city-registration-anmeldung.md), [05-bank-account.md](05-bank-account.md) (for SEPA/Kfz-Steuer and insurance payments).
**Unlocks:** Independent mobility around Nuremberg and Franconia — the alternative path is [12-public-transport.md](12-public-transport.md).

## Overview

Owning a car in Germany involves three separate stages that many newcomers don't realize are sequential and interdependent: **buy the car → get insurance first → register the car**. You cannot register a vehicle without an active insurance policy (specifically, an eVB-Nummer), and you cannot get a normal German insurance policy quote without knowing the exact vehicle. You also cannot register a car without a valid Anmeldung (address registration) already on file — see `04-city-registration-anmeldung.md`. This page supersedes the short note in `09-driving-license.md` and is the full treatment of buying, insuring, and registering a car in Nuremberg, plus ongoing obligations (TÜV, Kfz-Steuer, Umweltzone).

## Step-by-step process

### 1. Decide how to acquire the car

- **Private used-car purchase** — cheapest option, but the seller provides zero statutory warranty; you buy "as is" unless the contract says otherwise. Most common route for a first car in Germany.
- **Dealer (Gebrauchtwagenhändler / Vertragshändler)** — dealers are legally required to provide a minimum **one-year Sachmängelhaftung** (statutory liability for material defects) on used cars, giving you legal recourse if a defect appears. Prices are higher than private sales but you get consumer protection and often financing on-site.
- **Leasing** — common for new cars, especially through dealers/manufacturers; monthly payments with mileage limits and a return/buy-out at term end. Best for people who want a new car with predictable costs and don't want to deal with resale later; less common and less economical for a short-term stay.

### 2. If buying privately: verify the paperwork before paying

- **Kaufvertrag (purchase contract)** — get this in writing even for a cheap car. ADAC provides a free, widely-used Kaufvertrag template. It should include: full name/address of both parties, VIN (Fahrgestellnummer), make/model, current odometer reading (Kilometerstand), sale price, date of handover, and an "as is, no warranty" (unter Ausschluss jeglicher Gewährleistung) clause if the seller wants to exclude liability.
- **Zulassungsbescheinigung Teil I** (registration certificate, kept in the car — formerly "Fahrzeugschein") and **Teil II** (formerly "Fahrzeugbrief," proves ownership history) — the seller must hand over both. Check that the seller's name on Teil I/II matches their ID. Guard Teil II carefully; without it you cannot register the car.
- **TÜV / HU report and Mängelbericht** — check the expiry date of the current Hauptuntersuchung sticker/certificate. If it's expired or about to expire, factor the cost and hassle of a new inspection into your price negotiation or require the seller to have it done first.
- **Check for existing liens/debt** — a car can theoretically still be tied to a loan the previous owner hasn't finished paying off (Sicherungsübereignung), or the previous owner may not have properly deregistered it (Abmeldung). Ask directly whether the vehicle is fully paid off and lien-free, and if it was deregistered when the previous owner sold/stopped using it — if deregistered, get that deregistration document, as you'll need it to re-register.
- Consider a paid pre-purchase inspection (e.g., through ADAC) for anything beyond a trivial price — commonly cited around €90–150 (verify current figures).
- Pay by bank transfer, not cash, so there's a paper trail — cash deals are a common scam vector (fake bills, later payment disputes).

### 3. Financing (if not paying cash)

- **Bank loan (Autokredit)** — apply through your own bank or a comparison platform; typically requires Schufa credit history, which newcomers may not have yet (see `05-bank-account.md` and `10-taxes.md` for Schufa context).
- **Dealer financing** — arranged on the spot at purchase, often bundled with the dealer's own bank; convenient but compare the effective interest rate against a bank loan before signing.

### 4. Get insurance BEFORE you can register (mandatory sequencing)

You must have an active Kfz-Versicherung policy — specifically an **eVB-Nummer** — before the Zulassungsbehörde will register the car. Insurance types:

- **Kfz-Haftpflichtversicherung (third-party liability)** — legally mandatory for every registered vehicle in Germany; covers damage/injury you cause to others. This is the minimum required to register.
- **Teilkasko (partial comprehensive)** — optional; covers risks not caused by your own driving mistakes (theft, fire, glass breakage, storm/hail, collision with animals).
- **Vollkasko (full comprehensive)** — optional; includes everything Teilkasko covers plus damage to your own car even when the accident is your fault. Often required by lease/finance contracts on newer cars.
- **eVB-Nummer** — a short digital code your insurer issues within minutes once your policy is active; you give this number to the Zulassungsbehörde, which verifies coverage directly with the KBA (Federal Motor Transport Authority). Without it, registration cannot proceed.
- **Schadenfreiheitsklasse (SF-Klasse, no-claims class)** — this is the single biggest lever on your premium (can swing it by several hundred euros a year for identical coverage). Newcomers with no German insurance history are usually placed at the bottom, unrated tier (effectively SF 0), meaning you pay close to the full base premium in year one. **Before leaving your home country, request a formal no-claims/claims-history confirmation letter from your foreign insurer** — some German insurers will credit part of this history and meaningfully reduce your first-year premium; how much varies by insurer and is worth shopping around for.
- **Comparison sites** — Check24 and Verivox are the two dominant German insurance comparison platforms; get quotes from both before committing, and compare Haftpflicht-only vs. Teilkasko vs. Vollkasko pricing since the difference is sometimes smaller than expected.

### 5. Reserve your license plate(s) BEFORE the registration appointment

- Nuremberg plates carry the prefix "N". You can reserve a specific combination (Wunschkennzeichen) online through the city's portal or third-party services, typically holding the reservation for a limited window (commonly cited as 30–120 days depending on the service — verify).
- Many third-party "plate embossing" services (Kennzeichenpräger) let you reserve the combination online, have the physical plates embossed, and mail/hand them to you the same day — so you can walk into your Zulassungsstelle appointment with the plates already in hand. This is a common point of confusion: **you need the physical plates before or at the appointment, not after** — the registration clerk affixes the round TÜV/registration seal (Stempel/Plakette) onto the rear plate on the spot, which only makes sense if you already have the plate with you.
- Plate cost is typically small (see Costs below); a personalized combination usually carries a modest extra reservation fee.

### 6. Book and attend the Zulassungsbehörde appointment

- Book online as early as possible — see Nuremberg-specific office info below. Bring all documents (checklist below), the physical plates, and be prepared to sign a SEPA mandate for automatic Kfz-Steuer collection.
- The clerk registers the vehicle to you, stamps/seals the rear plate with the current HU-validity indicator if applicable, and issues you the updated Zulassungsbescheinigung Teil I.
- Processing itself is quick (commonly cited as 15–30 minutes) if your documents are complete — the bottleneck is almost always the appointment booking lead time, not the counter visit.

## Documents / Requirements checklist

- Valid ID: German Personalausweis, valid passport, or EU ID card
- Proof of registered address (Anmeldebestätigung / Meldebescheinigung) — must predate the registration appointment
- Valid driving license (see `09-driving-license.md` if converting a foreign license)
- Zulassungsbescheinigung Teil I and Teil II (from seller, for used cars) — or CoC (Certificate of Conformity) for a first-time EU registration of a vehicle imported from outside the EU
- eVB-Nummer from your German insurer
- Valid TÜV/HU certificate (current inspection, not expired)
- Physical license plates (embossed, plate reservation completed beforehand)
- SEPA mandate / German IBAN for Kfz-Steuer direct debit (a German-IBAN account from a digital bank such as N26 or a traditional bank both work)
- Signed Kaufvertrag (for your own records; not always required at the counter, but keep it)
- Deregistration document (Abmeldebescheinigung) if the previous owner had deregistered the car
- Power of attorney (Vollmacht) if someone else is registering on your behalf

## Costs (EUR — verify current figures)

- Used car price: highly variable; commonly cited as roughly one-third (or less) of the equivalent new car price
- New car average price: often cited around €40,000+ in recent years — verify current market figures
- Pre-purchase inspection (e.g., ADAC): roughly €90–150
- Registration fee (Zulassungsgebühr): roughly €26–60
- License plates: roughly €20–35 per plate (two plates for most cars); personalized combination reservation fee roughly €10–15 extra
- TÜV/HU inspection: roughly €80–130 depending on vehicle class and testing organization (TÜV, DEKRA, GTÜ, etc.)
- Kfz-Haftpflichtversicherung (liability only): roughly €300–600/year for an established driver with good SF class — **newcomers with no German claims history can pay significantly more in year one**; get quotes, don't assume a figure
- **Overall registration-day cost** (fees + plates, excluding insurance and any TÜV needed): commonly cited as landing somewhere around €150–350 total
- Kfz-Steuer (annual vehicle tax): calculated from engine displacement and CO2 emissions; petrol commonly cited around €2 per 100cc base rate, diesel around €9.50 per 100cc base rate, plus CO2-based surcharge — **verify current rates**, as this changes periodically. Fully electric vehicles are currently exempt (exemption reportedly runs through at least 2030 — verify).

## Timeline

- Anmeldung must be completed first (see `04-city-registration-anmeldung.md`) — car registration cannot proceed without it.
- Sourcing/negotiating a used car: days to a few weeks depending on how selective you are.
- Insurance quote and eVB-Nummer issuance: minutes to same-day once you've chosen a policy and provided the vehicle details.
- License plate reservation and embossing: same day to a couple of days via third-party plate services.
- Zulassungsbehörde appointment booking lead time: commonly cited as 1–4 weeks out in larger German cities, Nuremberg included — book as early as you know your target date.
- In-office registration processing: roughly 15–30 minutes if all documents are complete and correct.
- **Practical total timeline** from "found a car" to "legally registered and driving": often 1–3 weeks if you move efficiently and appointments aren't backlogged; longer if you need a TÜV inspection first or hit appointment scarcity.

## Common problems & issues

- **Newcomers hit with very high first-year insurance premiums** — with no German Schufa or German claims history, you're often placed at the bottom no-claims tier and charged close to the full base rate. Requesting a no-claims confirmation letter from your home-country insurer before you leave, and shopping Check24/Verivox aggressively, can meaningfully soften this.
- **Trying to register before Anmeldung is complete** — the most frequently cited stumbling block; the Zulassungsbehörde requires a valid Meldebescheinigung, so the sequence is always Anmeldung → insurance → registration, never the reverse.
- **Arriving at the appointment without physical plates** — a very common point of confusion for people used to systems where plates are issued at the registration office itself. In Germany you generally need the plates (or at least have them ordered/embossed and ready) before or at the appointment so the clerk can seal them on the spot.
- **Buying a car with a lien or an undischarged loan attached**, or one the previous owner never properly deregistered — always ask directly and get the deregistration document if applicable; a signed Kaufvertrag doesn't automatically clear an existing financial claim on the vehicle.
- **Private-sale disputes and scams** — cash-only sellers, mismatched names between seller and Teil I/II documents, odometer rollback, and sellers withholding Teil II after taking payment. Always transact by bank transfer, verify ID against the registration documents, and don't hand over money before receiving Teil II.
- **Expired TÜV discovered late** — buyers sometimes don't check the HU expiry date until the appointment, forcing a delay to get the car inspected before it can be registered (and it's illegal to drive on public roads with an expired HU in the meantime, beyond attention from enforcement).
- **Appointment backlogs** — Zulassungsstelle slots can be booked out 1–4 weeks in busier periods; plan around this rather than assuming a walk-in or next-day slot.
- **No German IBAN for the SEPA mandate** — resolved easily with any German-IBAN account (traditional bank or digital bank like N26), but catches people who haven't opened a German account yet (see `05-bank-account.md`).

## Benefits (of owning a car)

- Far more practical for trips into the surrounding Franconia region (day trips, hiking areas, smaller towns) where public transport frequency drops significantly outside the city.
- Flexibility for large grocery runs, moving furniture, weekend travel, and odd-hours travel when trains/buses run infrequently.
- Useful if you commute to a workplace outside Nuremberg's well-covered public transport corridors.

## Drawbacks (of owning a car)

- Nuremberg's public transport (U-Bahn, tram, bus, regional rail) is generally reliable and dense within the city — a car is often unnecessary for pure city living. See `12-public-transport.md` for that comparison in depth; this page only flags the trade-off.
- Ongoing costs stack up: insurance, Kfz-Steuer, TÜV every 1–2 years, fuel, parking, and repairs — meaningfully more expensive than a public transport pass for most single residents.
- City-center parking can be limited/metered (Anwohnerparken zones common); owning a car doesn't guarantee convenient parking near your home.
- Bureaucracy overhead (registration, deregistration if you leave, insurance switching) adds friction, especially for shorter stays.

## TÜV / Hauptuntersuchung (HU) details

- **Frequency**: new cars get their first HU after 3 years (36 months); after that, every 2 years for most passenger cars. (Some sources cite annual inspections for older commercial/heavier vehicles — verify for your specific vehicle class.)
- **AU (Abgasuntersuchung, emissions test)** is now folded into the HU as a combined inspection rather than a separate appointment — one inspection covers both roadworthiness and emissions.
- **Cost**: roughly €80–130 depending on vehicle class and inspection organization (TÜV, DEKRA, GTÜ, KÜS, etc. — all accredited, prices vary by provider).
- **Failing**: you get a Mängelbericht (defects report); you typically have about 4 weeks to fix the issues and return for a cheaper Nachprüfung (recheck) rather than paying for a full new inspection.
- **Driving with an expired HU**: it's an offense — commonly results in a fine, and if the vehicle has more serious deficiencies it can also carry demerit points; insurance complications can also arise if you're in an accident while overdue. Don't let it lapse.

## Umweltzone / environmental sticker (Umweltplakette)

- Nuremberg has a low-emission zone (Umweltzone) covering most of the city; **verify current boundaries and enforcement status**, as some German cities have relaxed or abolished their zones in recent years while others still enforce them.
- As of the 2025 nationwide simplification, red and yellow Umweltplakette stickers are no longer sold and are no longer valid for entry anywhere — effectively only the **green sticker** (Euro 4+ vehicles and cleaner) is valid for entry into any German low-emission zone now.
- Electric vehicles (and most plug-in hybrids) are generally exempt from Umweltzone restrictions.
- Fine for driving in the zone without the correct sticker is commonly cited around €80 — **verify current amount** before assuming it hasn't changed.
- If your car qualifies, get the green sticker (cheap, a few euros) at time of purchase/registration or from a TÜV/DEKRA station — don't assume it's automatically included with registration.

## Nuremberg-specific tips / office info

- **KFZ-Zulassungsstelle Nürnberg (vehicle registration office)**:
  - Address: Rathenauplatz 18, 90489 Nürnberg
  - Phone: 0911 / 231-0; Fax: 0911 / 231-4069; Email: oa1@stadt.nuernberg.de
  - Hours (verify before visiting): Mon 07:30–15:30, Tue 07:30–18:00, Wed 07:30–12:30, Thu 07:30–15:30, Fri 07:30–12:30
  - Reachable via U-Bahn lines U2/U3, Rathenauplatz stop; registered customers can use the parking garage at Sulzbacher Straße 16 free of charge.
  - Book appointments online via the city's Termine-Reservieren portal (nuernberg.de) — a separate appointment is needed for each distinct service (e.g., new registration vs. re-registration vs. deregistration); phone booking is often unavailable once online booking is live for a service.
- Reserve your Nuremberg ("N") Wunschkennzeichen plate combination online in advance, and use a same-day plate-embossing service if you want the physical plates in hand before your appointment — this avoids the common trap of showing up without plates.
- The Führerscheinstelle (driving license office) is a **separate office** from the Zulassungsbehörde — don't assume one appointment covers both driving license conversion and car registration; see `09-driving-license.md` for the Führerscheinstelle's details.
