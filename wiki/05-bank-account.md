# 05 — Opening a German Bank Account (Gehaltskonto)

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Main quest #5.
**Prerequisites:** [04-city-registration-anmeldung.md](04-city-registration-anmeldung.md) for most banks (some neobanks are more flexible — see below).
**Unlocks:** [06-health-insurance.md](06-health-insurance.md) payments, [16-liability-insurance.md](16-liability-insurance.md), postpaid mobile/internet contracts.

## Overview

Almost everything in Germany — rent, salary, Anmeldung-adjacent paperwork, health insurance direct debits, phone contracts, GEZ (broadcast fee) — expects a **German IBAN**. Foreign accounts (even EU SEPA ones) are sometimes rejected by landlords and employers, so opening a German account is one of the first practical tasks after (or even before) arriving in Nuremberg.

There are two broad paths:

- **Online/neobanks** (N26, DKB, Comdirect, bunq, Wise) — fast, English-friendly, many do **not** require an Anmeldung to open a basic account, verify identity via video-ident or NFC/passport scan.
- **Traditional branch banks** (Sparkasse Nürnberg, Deutsche Bank, Commerzbank, Postbank) — usually require an in-person visit, a Meldebescheinigung (proof of registered address), and sometimes a Schufa credit check; offer face-to-face German-language service and are often preferred by landlords/employers who are unfamiliar with neobanks.

Most newcomers to Nuremberg use a **hybrid strategy**: open a neobank account (N26/DKB) immediately on arrival to receive salary/pay rent, then later open or switch to a Sparkasse/traditional account once Anmeldung and Steuer-ID exist, if desired.

## Step-by-step process

1. **Before or immediately after arrival**: Open an online account (N26, DKB, bunq, or Wise) using just your passport — no Anmeldung needed. Complete video-ident or app-based NFC identity verification.
2. **Get a German SIM card** if possible before doing video-ident — some banking apps and video-ident providers have problems with foreign phone numbers/SMS codes (see Common Problems).
3. **Receive your first salary / pay first rent** into this account while you sort out Anmeldung.
4. **After Anmeldung**: Update your address with the bank, and/or open a second account with a traditional bank (Sparkasse Nürnberg, Deutsche Bank) if your employer, landlord, or personal preference requires it.
5. **Provide your Steuer-ID (tax ID)** to the bank once issued (usually a few weeks after Anmeldung) — required for interest reporting; can typically be submitted after account opening.
6. **For students needing a visa**: open a **blocked account (Sperrkonto)** *before* the visa appointment (see dedicated section below) — this is separate from a normal Girokonto.

## Documents / Requirements checklist

For a standard Girokonto (current account):

- [ ] Valid passport (all applicants) or EU national ID card (EU citizens)
- [ ] Visa / residence permit (Aufenthaltserlaubnis) — non-EU citizens; some banks want the physical card/stamp, not just the visa
- [ ] Meldebescheinigung / Anmeldung confirmation — required by most traditional banks (Sparkasse, Deutsche Bank, ING); **not** required by N26, bunq, Wise for a basic account
- [ ] Steuer-ID (tax ID) — often can be added after account opening
- [ ] Proof of income / employment contract — sometimes requested, especially for traditional banks or Schufa-based products (credit cards, overdraft)
- [ ] German or working phone number — needed for video-ident / app 2FA (verify current figures/requirements per bank)
- [ ] Schufa check — traditional banks may run one, especially for accounts with overdraft facilities; newcomers typically have no Schufa history, which is normal but can limit overdraft/credit card approval initially

## Costs (EUR — verify current figures)

- **N26**: Standard account free; paid tiers (Smart/Go/Metal) roughly €4.90 / €9.90 / €16.90 per month for extra features.
- **DKB**: Free if ≥€700 monthly incoming payments; otherwise around €4.50/month.
- **Comdirect**: Free Mastercard/Girocard on the standard current account; some interest paid on balances (rates change frequently).
- **Sparkasse Nürnberg**: Traditional fee-based Girokonto, typically a monthly account-management fee (varies by package) — check current Preis- und Leistungsverzeichnis on sparkasse-nuernberg.de.
- **Deutsche Bank**: Monthly fee unless minimum balance/income conditions met; verify current terms.
- **Blocked account (Sperrkonto)** — for students/job seekers:
  - Required deposit: reported around **€11,208–€11,904/year** (~€934–€992/month) for 2026 — figures vary by source; **verify the exact BAföG-linked figure for your visa year** with the embassy/BAMF.
  - **Expatrio**: ~€49 one-time setup fee, no monthly fee, often bundled with travel insurance.
  - **Fintiba**: ~€89 setup + ~€4.90/month.
  - **Deutsche Bank**: can also offer blocked accounts but usually requires an embassy/branch visit and is slower.

## Timeline

- Neobank account (N26/DKB): opened same day, card arrives by mail in a few business days to 2 weeks.
- Traditional bank (Sparkasse/Deutsche Bank): account opening appointment often needs to be booked in advance; card/PIN by mail take 1–2 weeks.
- Blocked account: Expatrio confirmation within ~24 hours of funds clearing; Fintiba immediate with card payment or 2–4 days for bank transfer.
- Activating blocked-account monthly payouts after arrival: requires Anmeldung + German bank account details first, then online update — a few days processing.

## Common problems & issues

- **Non-EU applicants rejected by branch banks**: some Sparkasse/Deutsche Bank branches are reluctant to open accounts for newcomers without Anmeldung or with only a visa (not yet a residence permit card); experience varies significantly by branch — try a different branch or use a neobank in the meantime.
- **Video-ident failures**: passport types not supported, poor lighting/connection, or the automated system flags document as invalid — fallback is usually Postident (verify in person at a post office) instead of video.
- **Needing a German phone number for banking apps/2FA before you have a German SIM**: chicken-and-egg problem for very new arrivals; some report success using their home-country number initially, but it's inconsistent — get a German SIM as early as possible (even a prepaid one from a kiosk/supermarket).
- **Landlords/employers refusing to accept neobank IBANs**: rare but reported on forums; a traditional Sparkasse/Deutsche Bank account can resolve this if it happens.
- **Waiting on Meldebescheinigung** delays traditional bank account opening — this is the single biggest bottleneck cited on liveingermany.de and expat forums.
- **Blocked account provider outages/pauses**: at least one provider (Coracle) has paused new applications before; always check current provider status before committing.
- **No Schufa history** limits credit card/overdraft approval initially — normal, resolves after a few months of German banking activity.

## Benefits

**Online/neobanks (N26, DKB, Comdirect, bunq, Wise)**
- Open without Anmeldung, fast, mostly English-language apps.
- Often free or low-cost basic accounts.
- Good for the immediate post-arrival gap before registration is complete.

**Traditional banks (Sparkasse Nürnberg, Deutsche Bank)**
- In-person, German-language support; large local branch/ATM network in Nuremberg.
- More readily accepted by conservative landlords, employers, and government offices.
- Easier path to build a long-term banking relationship with additional products (loans, mortgages).

## Drawbacks

**Online/neobanks**
- Purely app-based support; can be frustrating for complex issues or non-standard documentation.
- Some German institutions (a minority) still distrust or refuse to recognize N26/neobank IBANs.

**Traditional banks**
- Slower onboarding; usually require Anmeldung + in-branch appointment.
- Monthly fees more common; English service not guaranteed at every Sparkasse branch.

## Nuremberg-specific tips

- Sparkasse Nürnberg (sparkasse-nuernberg.de) has multiple branches across the city; service in English varies by branch — call ahead or check with the specific Filiale.
- If you need a Girokonto quickly while waiting for your Nuremberg Anmeldung appointment (which can have a multi-week wait), open N26 or DKB first — this is the most consistently recommended approach across expat guides.
- Deutsche Bank and Commerzbank both have branches in Nuremberg city center; some allow starting the online application before Anmeldung is confirmed, with a set window to supply it afterward — verify current policy directly with the branch.
