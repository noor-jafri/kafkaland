# Integrationskurs (Integration Course) — Nuremberg

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Main quest #8 — mandatory for some visa routes, voluntary for others.
**Prerequisites:** [07-auslanderbehorde-residence-permit.md](07-auslanderbehorde-residence-permit.md) (often where the obligation is confirmed).
**Unlocks:** [18-social-integration.md](18-social-integration.md) — the natural continuation once the mandatory course ends.

## Overview

The **Integrationskurs** is a state-funded German language and civics course administered by **BAMF** (Bundesamt für Migration und Flüchtlinge — Federal Office for Migration and Refugees). It combines intensive German language instruction (up to B1 level) with an **Orientierungskurs** covering German law, history, culture, and society. It is one of the largest adult education programs in Europe, with roughly 300,000+ participants per year nationwide (verify current figure).

## Who is required to take it

- **Mandatory (Verpflichtung) under §44a of the Residence Act** for many non-EU nationals whose German is below the level needed for daily life/integration — the Ausländerbehörde determines this at the time it issues your residence permit and issues you a **Berechtigungsschein** (entitlement/obligation certificate).
- Commonly mandated for:
  - **Family reunification / spouse visa holders** who don't already have sufficient German (A1 is required before arrival for most spouse visas; the Integrationskurs is often required afterward to reach B1).
  - Recipients of certain social benefits (e.g., Bürgergeld) if German proficiency is deemed insufficient.
  - Some cases identified by the Ausländerbehörde as needing "special integration need."
- **Voluntary** for:
  - EU/EEA citizens with insufficient German — they can apply directly to BAMF for a spot even though they're not obligated.
  - Work visa, EU Blue Card, job seeker, student, and freelance visa holders — generally not mandatory, but often taken voluntarily because B1 German is required later for permanent residency (Niederlassungserlaubnis) and citizenship, and passing the course's exams can substitute for those separate language/civics requirements.

If the course is mandatory and you don't participate (or drop out) without a valid reason, the Ausländerbehörde can shorten the validity of your residence permit, refuse to extend it, or in some cases impose sanctions on associated benefits — see "Consequences" below.

## Course structure

- **Total: 900 lessons of German language** (across six modules, A1 through B1), **plus 100 lessons (also cited as 45, depending on source/year — verify current standard) of Orientierungskurs** covering German legal system, history, and culture.
- Some sources describe the combined total as roughly **600–700+ hours of language instruction**; figures have changed over the years as BAMF revised the standard course model, so treat exact hour counts as approximate and verify against the current BAMF course description for the year you enroll.
- Special course formats exist: intensive courses (faster pace), literacy courses (for those who need to learn to read/write, up to ~1,300 lessons), courses for parents/women, youth integration courses, and courses for people with disabilities.
- Typical duration: **7–8 months** for a standard-pace course; intensive formats can finish in **5–6 months**. Part-time formats (a few days a week) can take longer — 9 to 24 months to reach B1 depending on schedule and provider.

## Finding a BAMF-approved provider in Nuremberg

- BAMF maintains **BAMF-NAvI**, an online course-finder tool where you search by postcode for certified providers (over 1,700 nationwide).
- In Nuremberg, the main public provider is the **Bildungszentrum Nürnberg**, which operates as the city's Volkshochschule (VHS) equivalent and runs both general Integrationskurse and Berufssprachkurse (job-related language courses). Participants can start a new course intake roughly up to seven times per year.
- Private/commercial providers also run BAMF-certified courses in Nuremberg (e.g., DAA Nürnberg, wbs training, sprachschule24), so compare schedules (morning/afternoon/evening) and start dates.
- Contact the chosen Kursträger (course provider) directly once you have your Berechtigungsschein or BAMF approval to register for a specific start date.

## Enrollment process

1. **Obtain permission to attend.** Either:
   - Get a **Berechtigungsschein** from the Ausländerbehörde when your permit is issued (mandatory case), or
   - **Apply directly to BAMF** if you're voluntary (e.g., EU citizen, or a permit holder wanting to attend without an obligation).
2. **Search BAMF-NAvI by postcode** for approved course providers near you in Nuremberg.
3. **Contact the provider** to check available start dates/times and confirm a place — popular providers can have waitlists.
4. **Register with required documents** — valid ID/passport, residence permit (should generally be valid for at least 6 months at registration), and your Berechtigungsschein or BAMF approval letter.
5. **Attend a placement/orientation session** — some providers test your existing German level to place you in the correct module (A1–B1).
6. Pay the module fee in advance (see Costs) unless you qualify for a fee waiver/reduction.

## Exams at the end

- **Deutsch-Test für Zuwanderer (DTZ):** Tests speaking, listening, reading, and writing; certifies whether you've reached A2 or B1 level. If you pass, you receive the "Zertifikat Integrationskurs."
- **Leben in Deutschland Test:** A civics/orientation exam — commonly cited as 33 multiple-choice questions (drawn from a larger official question pool, often cited as 310 questions nationally plus state-specific questions); you need at least **17 correct answers** to pass.
- If you fail the DTZ, you may be permitted to repeat part of the course (commonly cited as up to 300 additional lesson units) and retake the exam.
- Passing both exams within a set timeframe (commonly cited as **within two years** of starting) can qualify you for a **50% refund** of the course fee you paid, and successful completion is one of the fastest routes to satisfying the German-language and civics requirements for both Niederlassungserlaubnis and citizenship applications.

## Costs (EUR, verify current figures)

- **€2.22 per lesson unit** is a commonly cited current BAMF-subsidized rate (verify — this rate is periodically adjusted).
- Each module (100 lesson units) costs roughly **€220–€230** at the subsidized rate — Bildungszentrum Nürnberg's published module fee is cited around **€229**.
- Full course (all modules + orientation course) at the subsidized rate: roughly **€1,900–€2,000 total** (verify against current total lesson count).
- **Full-cost (non-subsidized) participants** — e.g., some voluntary participants not eligible for the subsidy — pay a higher per-lesson rate; check with BAMF/provider for the current unsubsidized price.
- **Fee waivers/reductions:**
  - Fully waived for recipients of **Bürgergeld** (basic income support) and some other social benefit recipients.
  - Low-income participants can apply to BAMF for a full or partial cost exemption (Kostenbefreiung) even without receiving benefits.
  - **50% refund** of fees paid if you pass the final exams within the allotted timeframe.
- Travel cost subsidies are also available for participants who must travel a significant distance to their course provider — apply via BAMF's online portal.

## Timeline

- Registration to course start: depends on provider waitlist — can range from a few weeks to a few months in a city with limited slots.
- Course duration: **5–8 months** typical for standard/intensive pace; up to **24 months** for slower part-time formats.
- Exam and certificate issuance: shortly after course completion, assuming no retakes needed.
- Fee refund (if applicable): processed after passing exams within the 2-year window.

## Common problems & issues

- **Long waitlists for popular providers** in Nuremberg, especially for courses at convenient times (mornings/evenings) or with childcare-friendly schedules — a widely reported issue at Integrationskurs providers across German cities generally.
- **Childcare during the course** — since standard courses run daily on a fixed schedule for months, parents (especially those on family reunification visas) report difficulty finding course times compatible with school/daycare hours; some providers offer courses specifically designed for parents but slots may be limited.
- **Conflicts with work schedules** — full-time workers can struggle to attend daytime intensive courses; evening or part-time course options exist but are less widely available and take longer to complete.
- **Pace gaps between participants** — mixed-level or mixed-age classrooms (younger vs. older learners, or highly literate vs. those needing literacy support) can slow progress for some and frustrate others; personal accounts recommend supplementing with home study and conversation practice (e.g., Stammtisch groups).
- **Failing the DTZ or Leben in Deutschland exam** and needing to retake — costs additional time and, in some cases, additional lesson fees for repeated modules.
- **System/administrative bottlenecks** — reports (including a 2026 correctiv.org report) of BAMF at times pausing new course-provider approvals/authorizations, which can further tighten already-limited course capacity in some regions.
- **Confusion over obligation status** — some permit holders are unsure whether they are actually obligated to attend or only eligible voluntarily, since the Berechtigungsschein/obligation notice isn't always clearly explained at the Ausländerbehörde appointment.

## Benefits

- Reaching B1 German is a prerequisite (or accelerator) for permanent residency and citizenship — completing the course is often the most structured, affordable way to get there.
- Subsidized cost (as low as free for many participants) makes it one of the cheapest formal ways to learn German to B1 in Germany.
- The Orientierungskurs provides practical grounding in German law, history, and civic life that helps with everyday bureaucracy and social integration.
- Successful completion can shorten the path to citizenship under recent Nationality Act reforms and unlock a partial fee refund.
- Structured daily practice accelerates language acquisition faster than self-study for most learners.

## Drawbacks

- Time-intensive — months of near-daily commitment that can be hard to combine with full-time work or childcare.
- Waitlists mean you may not be able to start immediately even when motivated and eligible.
- Mixed-ability classrooms can mean uneven pacing.
- Failing exams means repeating lesson blocks, adding more months and (potentially) more fees.
- Mandatory participants who don't attend risk real consequences (see below), adding pressure for those already juggling work, family, and settling-in stress.

## Consequences of not completing when mandatory

- The Ausländerbehörde can take non-attendance or non-completion into account when deciding on **residence permit extension** — it can be a factor in refusing an extension or shortening the permit's validity.
- For those on social benefits, non-participation without a valid excuse can lead to benefit reductions/sanctions under German social law.
- Failing to reach B1 (or the required level for your permit type) can delay eligibility for permanent residency (Niederlassungserlaubnis) and naturalization, since course completion or an equivalent language certificate is generally required for both.
- Valid exemptions exist (e.g., health reasons, caregiving duties, already having sufficient German) — these should be raised with the Ausländerbehörde/BAMF rather than simply not attending.

## Nuremberg-specific tips / office info

- **Main public provider:** Bildungszentrum Nürnberg (VHS Nürnberg equivalent) — runs BAMF Integrationskurse and Berufssprachkurse; module fee cited around €229; new intakes offered up to ~7 times per year.
- **Course finder:** Use BAMF-NAvI (BAMF's official geo-search tool) filtered by Nuremberg postcodes to see all currently certified providers, not just the Bildungszentrum.
- **Other Nuremberg providers found in research:** DAA Nürnberg, wbstraining, sprachschule24 — compare start dates and schedule formats (morning/afternoon/online-hybrid).
- Given reported capacity constraints, **register as early as possible** after receiving your Berechtigungsschein rather than waiting, especially if you need a specific schedule (e.g., for childcare or work compatibility).
- If mandatory and struggling to get a timely slot, communicate proactively with your Ausländerbehörde caseworker in Nuremberg to document that the delay is due to provider capacity, not personal non-compliance — this can matter for permit extension decisions.
