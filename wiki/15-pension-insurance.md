# Pension Insurance (Rentenversicherung)

> Last researched: 2026-07-23. Verify current rules/fees before relying on this, as German bureaucracy changes frequently.

**Sequence:** Side quest — automatic for employees, opt-in decision for freelancers.
**Prerequisites:** [06-health-insurance.md](06-health-insurance.md) (social security registration overlaps), employment contract.
**Unlocks:** Statutory pension entitlement in Germany; relevant to long-term settlement planning.

## Overview

If you're employed in Germany (not self-employed/freelance in most cases), you are automatically enrolled in the statutory pension insurance system (Deutsche Rentenversicherung) as part of German social security.

## Process

- Your employer registers you and deducts your share of pension contributions directly from your gross salary, matching it with an employer contribution.
- You're issued a **Rentenversicherungsnummer** (pension insurance number), also called the Sozialversicherungsnummer (SV-Nummer) — a 12-digit number that tracks your contributions across your entire working life in Germany. It's issued once, by Deutsche Rentenversicherung, and used on payslips and HR paperwork.
- If you don't have one yet when starting your first job, your employer's health insurer typically requests one on your behalf.

## Costs (verify current figures)

- Pension contribution rate is roughly 18.6% of gross salary, split roughly 50/50 between employee and employer (so around 9.3% comes out of your paycheck) — verify current rate, as social security contribution rates are adjusted periodically.

## Common problems & issues

- Non-EU nationals sometimes assume contributions are "lost" if they leave Germany — in some cases (depending on bilateral social security agreements with your home country) contributions can be refunded or count toward pensions abroad, but rules are country-specific and complex.
- Losing track of your Rentenversicherungsnummer across multiple jobs/name changes is common; it can be recovered by contacting Deutsche Rentenversicherung directly.
- Freelancers/self-employed people are generally **not** automatically enrolled and need to actively decide whether to opt in — easy to miss if coming from an employee mindset.

## Benefits

- Fully automatic for employees — no paperwork burden beyond your first job's onboarding.
- Contributions may be portable or refundable depending on your home country's social security agreement with Germany.
- Builds toward a German state pension the longer you stay and work.

## Drawbacks

- Freelancers must actively research and opt in — there's no default safety net if you don't.
- Cross-border portability is genuinely complex and country-specific, making long-term planning harder for people unsure whether they'll stay in Germany permanently.

## Tips

- Keep your Rentenversicherungsnummer somewhere safe (it appears on your payslip/Lohnabrechnung and social security card).
- If you plan to leave Germany permanently, look into whether your home country has a social security agreement with Germany before assuming you'll get contributions refunded.
