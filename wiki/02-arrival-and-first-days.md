# Arrival and First Days in Nuremberg

## Overview

This file covers the practical steps for the first days and weeks after landing in Germany, whether you fly directly into Nuremberg Airport (NUE) or arrive via a larger hub like Frankfurt or Munich and travel onward. It applies to all visa types covered in `01-before-you-arrive.md` — the sequence of "must-do-fast" bureaucratic steps (Anmeldung, bank account, tax ID, health insurance, Ausländerbehörde) is largely the same regardless of your visa category, though non-EU visa holders have the added step of converting their entry visa into a residence permit.

## Step-by-step process

### 1. Arriving via Nuremberg Airport (NUE)
1. Nuremberg Albrecht Dürer Airport (NUE) is small and connects mainly to European destinations plus some hub connections.
2. From the airport, take the **U-Bahn line U2**, which runs directly between Nürnberg Flughafen and Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof (Main Station) in about 12 minutes, running up to 6 times/hour from roughly 4am to past midnight.
3. A single ticket (Tariff Zone A / "Preisstufe A") costs around €3.40 (verify current VGN fare) and is valid 90 minutes with transfers.
4. Buses (line 32 to Thon, line 33 to Nürnberg-Nord) are an alternative if your accommodation isn't near the U-Bahn line.
5. A taxi to the city center costs roughly €25 and takes about 15–20 minutes.

### 2. Arriving via Frankfurt or Munich and traveling to Nuremberg
1. Frankfurt Airport (FRA) and Munich Airport (MUC) are the two major international hubs with far more long-haul connections than NUE.
2. From Frankfurt: take a Deutsche Bahn (DB) long-distance train (ICE) from Frankfurt Airport's long-distance train station (Fernbahnhof) directly to Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof — journey time is roughly 2 hours.
3. From Munich: DB ICE trains run frequently from Munich Hauptbahnhof to Nürnberg Hauptbahnhof, journey time roughly 1–1.5 hours (you'll need to get from Munich Airport to Munich Hauptbahnhof first via S-Bahn, about 40 minutes).
4. Book DB tickets in advance via bahn.de or the DB Navigator app for better prices; prices rise closer to departure.
5. With heavy luggage, consider a door-to-door shuttle/taxi service instead of trains with transfers.

### 3. First-days checklist (roughly week 1)
1. Check into temporary accommodation (see below) if you don't yet have a permanent flat.
2. Get a local SIM card or activate an eSIM (see below) so you have a working German phone number for calls/appointments.
3. Do a first grocery run for essentials — note Sunday closing hours (see below).
4. Locate the nearest Bürgeramt (citizens' office) branch and book your Anmeldung appointment as soon as you know your address — do this immediately, not after settling in, given typical wait times.
5. Note the emergency numbers **112** (fire/ambulance) and **110** (police) — both free, work without a SIM, and operators generally speak English.
6. If applicable, note your appointment date/reference for the Ausländerbehörde (residence permit) — booking this early is critical given Nuremberg's reported long backlogs.

### 4. Immediate priorities checklist (weeks 1–4) — why Anmeldung must happen fast
The dependency chain in Germany is strict and sequential:
1. **Anmeldung (address registration)** — legally required within **14 days** of moving into permanent housing. This is the foundation: almost nothing else can proceed without it.
2. **Tax ID (Steuer-ID)** — automatically generated after Anmeldung and mailed to your registered address within 2–4 weeks. Without it on file, employers must withhold tax at the highest bracket (Steuerklasse VI, reported around 42% withholding) until it's provided.
3. **Bank account** — many banks want the Steuer-ID, though some let you open an account first and add the Steuer-ID later. Needed for salary deposits, rent payments, and utility contracts.
4. **Health insurance enrollment (public/private)** — needed to finalize employment contracts and, for non-EU nationals, to move from visa-stage travel insurance to full statutory/private coverage.
5. **Residence permit conversion (non-EU only)** — book the Ausländerbehörde appointment as early as your visa allows; Nuremberg specifically has reported multi-month-to-multi-year backlogs (see Common Problems below).

Missing the 14-day Anmeldung deadline can, per German federal law (§ 54 Bundesmeldegesetz), result in a fine of up to €1,000, though enforcement in practice varies and first-time newcomers are often treated leniently.

## Documents/Requirements checklist

For the Anmeldung appointment (Bürgeramt), bring:
- Valid passport or national ID card
- Completed Anmeldeformular (registration form — pre-fill online from the city's portal where possible)
- **Wohnungsgeberbestätigung** (landlord confirmation of move-in — mandatory since 2015; request this from your landlord in writing immediately if delayed)
- Marriage certificate (if registering with a spouse) and children's birth certificates (if registering minors)
- Visa/residence permit (non-EU nationals)

For opening a bank account:
- Passport/ID
- Anmeldebescheinigung (registration certificate) — required by most banks
- Proof of income/employment contract (for some banks/account types)

For a local SIM card:
- Passport/ID for the mandatory Video-Ident registration process (required by German law for all SIM registrations)
- German address helps for postpaid contracts but is not required for prepaid

## Costs (EUR — verify current figures)

- Anmeldung itself: free
- U-Bahn/tram/bus single ticket (Nuremberg VGN network, Tariff Zone A): ~€3.40
- Taxi from NUE airport to city center: ~€25
- ICE train Frankfurt Airport → Nuremberg or Munich → Nuremberg: roughly €30–90 one-way depending on how far in advance booked (verify current DB pricing)
- Prepaid SIM card starter pack: roughly €10–20 including some credit
- Temporary accommodation (Airbnb/serviced apartment/hostel) in Nuremberg: highly variable, roughly €30–50/night for hostels, €60–150+/night for serviced apartments/Airbnb — verify current listings
- First grocery shop for basic essentials: roughly €30–60 for a single person's initial stock-up (Aldi/Lidl typically cheapest, Rewe/Edeka mid-range)

## Timeline / how long it takes

- Anmeldung: legally must happen within 14 days of move-in; the appointment itself and the issued Meldebescheinigung (registration certificate) are typically same-day once you have an appointment slot — but *booking* that appointment can itself take days to weeks in busy cities, so book as soon as you have a fixed address, ideally before or immediately upon arrival.
- Tax ID: arrives by post 2–4 weeks after Anmeldung.
- Bank account: can often be opened within the first 1–2 weeks, sometimes before the Steuer-ID is issued depending on the bank.
- Residence permit conversion / Ausländerbehörde appointment (non-EU): in Nuremberg specifically, reported wait times have ranged from several months to well over a year for a first appointment, and full case resolution has taken up to ~17 months in reported cases (see `01-before-you-arrive.md` for details) — plan your legal-stay documentation accordingly and keep records of your application date.
- Finding permanent housing: commonly weeks to a couple of months in competitive German rental markets; using temporary accommodation as a bridge is standard practice.

## Common problems & issues people face

- **Landlord delays on the Wohnungsgeberbestätigung:** Some landlords are slow to issue this required confirmation, holding up the Anmeldung appointment. Tip reported by expats: request it in writing by email immediately to create a paper trail.
- **Appointment scarcity for Anmeldung:** In busy cities, appointment slots can be booked out weeks in advance; checking the online booking portal early morning for cancellations is a commonly cited workaround.
- **Nuremberg's Ausländerbehörde backlog:** Widely reported as one of the slowest in Bavaria — residents describe the digital appointment system as frustrating, with no email contact and months of silence after applying. Only appointments via the "Mein Nürnberg" portal are accepted; walk-ins are turned away.
- **Sunday closures catching newcomers off guard:** Because of the Ladenschlussgesetz (shop closing law), most retail including grocery stores is closed on Sundays and public holidays (with exceptions like train station shops, gas stations, and a few 24-hour convenience spots). Arriving on a Sunday without groceries is a commonly reported early mistake — food delivery apps are the usual workaround.
- **Noise rules on Sundays:** Loud housework (vacuuming, drilling, lawn mowing) on Sundays and during designated quiet hours is discouraged and can draw complaints from neighbors — a common culture-shock point for newcomers.
- **Cash-preference culture:** Many smaller shops, bakeries, and restaurants in Germany (including Bavaria) still prefer or require cash; some card machines don't accept foreign or certain card types. Carrying cash is commonly recommended, especially in the first weeks before a German bank account/card is active.
- **Slow restaurant service by design:** Waitstaff generally won't bring the check until asked — a frequently cited culture-shock/adjustment point, not poor service.
- **Tax withholding shock:** Until the Steuer-ID is registered with an employer, salary is taxed at the highest bracket (Steuerklasse VI), which can be a shock on a first payslip — this resolves once the ID is filed.
- **SIM registration friction:** The mandatory ID verification (Video-Ident) for SIM cards trips up some new arrivals unfamiliar with the process; having your passport ready and good lighting/internet for the video call helps it go smoothly.

## Benefits

- Nuremberg is well-connected by both a small local airport (NUE, quick regional/European links) and fast ICE rail links to Frankfurt and Munich hub airports, giving flexible arrival options.
- The city's public transport (U-Bahn, tram, bus network run by VGN) is efficient and makes the airport-to-city and general first-days logistics straightforward.
- A large stock of temporary accommodation (Airbnb, serviced apartments, extended-stay platforms like Wunderflats/HousingAnywhere) makes bridging the gap before securing a permanent flat manageable.
- Emergency services (112/110) are reliable, free, and generally accessible in English.

## Drawbacks

- Nuremberg's Ausländerbehörde has a reputation (per resident and press reports) as one of the more backlogged immigration offices in Bavaria, which can leave non-EU newcomers in bureaucratic limbo for a long stretch after arrival.
- Strict Sunday/holiday retail closures and quiet-hours norms require real adjustment for people used to 24/7 retail cultures.
- The sequential bureaucracy (Anmeldung → Steuer-ID → bank account → insurance → residence permit) means a single early delay (e.g., a slow landlord confirmation) can cascade into delays across every subsequent step.
- Airport-wise, NUE has limited long-haul connections, meaning many newcomers must transit through Frankfurt or Munich with extra luggage-handling and train transfers.

## Nuremberg-specific tips and locations

- **Airport transport:** U-Bahn U2 direct to Hauptbahnhof, ~12 minutes, ~€3.40 (Tariff Zone A) — verify current VGN fares.
- **Bürgeramt for Anmeldung:** main office reported at **Burgstraße 4, 90403 Nürnberg**; book online via the city's "Mein Nürnberg" service portal — walk-in waits can be long, so booking ahead is strongly recommended.
- **Ausländerbehörde (foreigners'/immigration office):** **Regensburger Str. 231, 90478 Nürnberg**, phone 0911/231-0; appointment-only via "Mein Nürnberg" portal.
- **Temporary accommodation while flat-hunting:** Airbnb and serviced-apartment listings are concentrated in the Mitte (city center) district; Wunderflats, HousingAnywhere, and The Homelike are commonly recommended platforms for furnished/short-term stays that bridge into longer leases.
- **Grocery basics:** Common chains in Nuremberg include Lidl, Aldi, Edeka, Rewe, and Kaufland. Most operate roughly 7am–8pm on weekdays with reduced Saturday hours and closure on Sundays; a Rewe To Go location at Bahnhofsplatz 9 near the Hauptbahnhof has been reported as operating 24 hours — useful for Sunday/late-night emergencies (verify current hours before relying on it).
- **SIM card:** pick up prepaid top-up vouchers at Rewe, Edeka, Lidl, or Aldi checkouts, or order an eSIM/prepaid SIM online in advance (Telekom-based providers like Congstar or Lidl Connect are often recommended for the best rural/regional coverage around Nuremberg and Bavaria).
